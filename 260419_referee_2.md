# Referee Report on “Discipline Without Creative Destruction? Import Competition and Market Power in Turkey”

## Summary and Main Contribution

The thesis studies how rising Chinese import competition affects the dynamics of market power in Turkish manufacturing, with a focus on disentangling output‑competition and input‑supply channels in an oligopolistic environment with variable markups and endogenous exit. The author combines a Cournot‑style model with nested CES demand and input–output linkages with firm‑level data from ORBIS/AMADEUS and sectoral data from CEPII BACI, TURKSTAT, WIOD, and UNIDO over 2009–2023. A shift‑share IV based on Chinese export growth to high‑income markets (EU, US, Japan, Australia) provides identification for the output‑competition channel, while an input‑supply shifter is constructed by weighting this IV with Turkish input–output linkages.[^1]

On the empirical side, there are four pillars: (i) firm‑level IV regressions of markup changes on Chinese import penetration, (ii) a flexible selection correction motivated by Ericson–Pakes and Backus (2020), (iii) a decomposition of sectoral markups into within, between (reallocation), entry, and exit components using harmonic‑mean aggregation, and (iv) grouped IV quantile regressions to study distributional heterogeneity. The main findings are that: (a) final‑goods import penetration significantly reduces markup growth at the firm level, with effects concentrated among high‑markup/high‑share firms in more concentrated sectors; (b) the input‑supply channel has little direct effect on markups but reallocates market shares toward lower‑markup firms with stronger input linkages; (c) selection effects are empirically small, so the pro‑competitive impact of trade is mainly via incumbent adjustment; and (d) at the sector level, within‑firm and reallocation components dominate entry/exit, but the overall change in sectoral markups is modest.[^1]

Overall, the question is important and well motivated, at the intersection of quantitative trade, industrial organization, and development, and the thesis is ambitious for an M2 project. Theoretical and empirical work are integrated in a coherent way, and the identification strategy is sophisticated and largely in line with recent trade literature. At the same time, some aspects of the exposition, identification arguments, and empirical implementation could be tightened to improve credibility and readability.

## Major Comments

### 1. Clarity and discipline of the theoretical framework

The theoretical chapter develops a Cournot oligopoly model with nested CES preferences (between sectors, between domestic vs foreign, and across domestic varieties), endogenous markups, and input–output linkages affecting marginal cost. The model delivers an inverse‑markup expression in terms of sectoral concentration and domestic expenditure shares and then introduces a dynamic extension with endogenous exit through a value‑function and a selection threshold.[^1]

The framework is rich, but in its current form it is somewhat dense for a reader trying to understand what is essential for the later empirics.

- Consider tightening the model to a clearer “minimal backbone”: explicitly distinguish which elements are truly needed to justify (i) the sign predictions on the output‑competition channel, (ii) the ambiguity of the input‑supply channel for markups, and (iii) the decomposition of sectoral markups. Some intermediate derivations (e.g. full Bellman exposition) could be shortened or moved to the appendix.[^1]
- The conditions on the elasticities \(ρ_j, ν_j, η\) and their mapping to the comparative statics (Prediction 2) are important but currently easy to miss. Spell out more clearly how the inequalities imply that the pro‑competitive effect is stronger for large firms and more concentrated sectors, perhaps with a simple diagram or numeric example.[^1]
- The dynamic extension is conceptually helpful but not tightly integrated with the estimation strategy. The selection correction later uses lagged EBIT margin as a one‑dimensional proxy for the latent state, while the model’s state vector includes productivity, demand, lagged share, and trade shocks. It would strengthen the narrative to explain more explicitly why a single profitability proxy is a reasonable sufficient statistic in practice, and to acknowledge that this is a reduced‑form approximation rather than a structural selection correction.[^1]

### 2. Identification and the shift–share IV design

The China‑shock identification follows Autor et al. and Borusyak et al., with a sector‑level shift built from Chinese export growth to high‑income destinations and shares given by baseline Chinese import penetration in Turkey. The thesis also constructs an input‑supply shifter by propagating the output IV through input–output linkages.[^1]

This is broadly standard and appropriate, but several refinements could improve the credibility of the exclusion restriction and help the reader assess how much identifying variation is left after controls.

- Exclusion restriction and potential confounders: The placebo tests with leads of the instrument are useful and show no strong pre‑trends in markup changes. However, the discussion could be more explicit about what kinds of shocks would violate the exclusion restriction in this environment (e.g. sector‑specific global demand shocks that simultaneously raise Chinese exports to high‑income markets and Turkish markups). A short subsection spelling out “threats to identification” and the role of sector and time fixed effects would be valuable.[^1]
- Granularity: The Rotemberg‑weight robustness shows that a small number of sectors carry a lot of influence, and excluding them weakens the first stage. This is informative, but it would help to be upfront in the main text that the identifying variation is granular and oligopolistic by construction: a few large sectors drive the results. You could rephrase this as a feature rather than a bug, but then interpret external validity accordingly.[^1]
- Construction of the input‑supply shifter: The upstream shifter is built by weighting the output IV with 2003 Turkish input–output coefficients and Chinese source shares. Given the long sample (2009–2023), it would be useful to discuss how stable these network linkages likely are over time and whether the use of a single baseline matrix could bias the magnitude of the input‑supply channel. Even if dynamic IO data are unavailable, a brief sensitivity check (e.g. alternative normalizations or trimming high‑weight links) would reassure the reader.[^1]
- Level vs changes: The main treatment is the change in import penetration, instrumented by the shift–share. The text sometimes mixes level and change terminology. Ensure that all regression equations, tables, and descriptive passages consistently emphasize that the instrument relates to changes, and clarify whether any fixed‑effect structure would absorb levels in practice.[^1]

### 3. Markup measurement and robustness

The thesis uses De Loecker–Warzynski–style revenue‑based markups using COGS as the flexible input, complemented by robustness work using Kirov et al. (2026).[^1]

- Data limitations and measurement error: The discussion already acknowledges concerns about revenue‑based markups, and the main analysis focuses on changes in log markup rather than levels. It would still help to be more transparent about the quality of ORBIS accounting data in Turkey (coverage, reporting practices, and trimming rules) and to show more clearly how the winsorization of extreme markup changes affects distributional results.[^1]
- Choice of flexible input: You use COGS as the flexible input and estimate the output elasticity to COGS in a production function framework. It could be useful to explain briefly why other inputs (e.g. materials vs labor) are not preferred in this setting, especially given the later focus on imported intermediates. Even a short robustness table using an alternative input share as a proxy could strengthen confidence.[^1]
- Kirov et al. robustness: The Kirov‑style estimates are based on a substantially smaller sample, leading to imprecision. The current text correctly cautions about this but could be more explicit in the main empirical chapter that these robustness checks are illustrative rather than decisive; otherwise, there is a slight risk that readers over‑interpret the similarity of point estimates given the sample change.[^1]

### 4. Selection correction and interpretation of “small” selection

The selection‑correction strategy is an important and original element of the thesis. You use lagged EBIT margin as a proxy for the latent profitability state, show that it predicts exit and markup adjustments, and then include a flexible polynomial in this proxy in the second‑stage markup regression. The corrected coefficient on import penetration is similar to the uncorrected one, leading to the conclusion that selection is small.[^1]

- Interpretation: The similarity of coefficients suggests that conditioning on lagged profitability does not drastically change the estimated within‑firm effect, but this does not imply that selection is unimportant in an economic sense. A more cautious wording would be to say that “conditional on the proxy, the within‑incumbent effect remains robust,” and that the decomposition later shows that entry and exit margins are small relative to within and reallocation components.[^1]
- Proxy validity: Table 2 shows that lagged EBIT margin is persistent, predicts exit, and correlates with future markup changes; the Markov test finds that adding a second lag does not significantly improve predictive power. These are good diagnostics. It would be helpful to add a short discussion of potential remaining biases—for example, if trade shocks themselves affect EBIT margin in ways not fully captured by the control function, or if other state variables (e.g. leverage, age) matter for exit but are imperfectly captured.[^1]
- Robustness: You briefly mention alternative selection controls (lagged markup level and size proxies) in the robustness section. Bringing at least one of these robustness tables into the main text, or summarizing the key result (that the betas remain negative and of similar magnitude) would strengthen the claim that the selection correction is not sensitive to the exact choice of state proxy.[^1]

### 5. Firm‑to‑sector aggregation and the role of reallocation

The decomposition of sectoral markups into within, reallocation, entry, and exit components—using the harmonic mean and Melitz–Polanec‑style decomposition—is a strong point of the thesis. The finding that within and reallocation each account for roughly half of the absolute change in inverse sectoral markups, while entry/exit are negligible, is interesting.[^1]

However, the interpretation could be sharpened.

- Signs and magnitudes: In Table 5, the estimated coefficient of import penetration on the total sectoral markup change is small and imprecise, while the input‑supply shock significantly raises inverse markups via reallocation. It would be useful to discuss more explicitly the mapping from these inverse‑markup changes to consumer surplus and welfare, at least qualitatively. The current text emphasizes that trade liberalization and antitrust policy can be substitutes, but the welfare angle is not fully developed.[^1]
- Dominance of incumbents: Panel B shows that within‑firm and reallocation responses among incumbents account for essentially all of the adjustment. This is a key result that deserves more space in the conclusion: it suggests that, in this context, “creative destruction” via entry and exit is limited, and most adjustment happens through changing behavior and market‑share reallocation among surviving firms.[^1]
- Relation to distributional results: The grouped IV quantile estimates reveal that pro‑competitive effects are concentrated in the upper tail of the markup‑adjustment distribution, especially in more concentrated sectors. It would be helpful to connect this more explicitly to the reallocation pattern: are the firms in the upper tail also those losing or gaining shares in the sectoral decomposition? A short descriptive table linking quantile groups to size or share dynamics could clarify this.[^1]

### 6. Exposition, redundancy, and length

The manuscript is long for an M2 thesis, with extensive appendices and multiple robustness tables. The level of ambition is commendable, but there are places where tightening would improve readability without sacrificing substance.[^1]

- Introduction: The introduction clearly states the research question and contributions but is dense, with long paragraphs and repeated statements about the importance of oligopoly and emerging markets. Consider restructuring into shorter paragraphs that separate (i) motivation and context, (ii) main empirical findings, and (iii) contributions to the literatures.[^1]
- Empirical strategy: Section 4 sometimes repeats information already described in data and theory (e.g. treatment variables, high‑level description of the IV). You could trim descriptive passages and focus on what is specific to each sub‑section (baseline IV, selection correction, decomposition, distributional design).[^1]
- Robusstness section: The robustness section is thorough but could be more clearly signposted. A short roadmap at the beginning (listing the main types of robustness) would help readers navigate. Some more technical details (e.g. specific wild‑bootstrap implementation issues) can likely be kept in the appendix.

## Minor Comments and Suggestions

### Writing, notation, and structure

- Typographical issues: The current PDF shows many missing spaces due to extraction (e.g. “oligopolyiswidespread”). Ensure the final version has proper spacing and check for small typos in variable names and subscripts.[^1]
- Equation referencing: Some key equations (e.g. the inverse‑markup expression and the decomposition formula) deserve explicit equation numbers in the main text, since later discussion refers to them informally. This would help the reader cross‑reference predictions with empirical counterparts.[^1]
- Units and scaling: For variables like import penetration and markup changes, clarify the units (percentage points vs log points) more consistently in text and tables.[^1]

### Data and descriptive statistics

- Sector coverage: It would help to add a short descriptive table or figure showing the distribution of sector sizes, concentration measures, and Chinese import exposure across sectors at baseline. This would give the reader more intuition about where the identifying variation lies.[^1]
- Time dimension: Given the long panel (2009–2023), a simple graph of average import penetration and average markups over time for Turkish manufacturing would help contextualize the regression results.[^1]
- Trimming rules: Summarize more explicitly how many observations are dropped at each trimming step (e.g. COGS, negative revenue, thinning extremes of markup changes) and whether dropped firms differ systematically from those retained.[^1]

### Empirical implementation details

- Fixed‑effect structure: Ensure that every regression table clearly states whether firm, sector, and/or year fixed effects are included. In some appendix tables this is implicit.[^1]
- Inference: Where wild‑cluster bootstrap p‑values are available and differ from conventional clustered standard errors, highlight them briefly in the text, especially for borderline coefficients.[^1]
- Alternative outcomes: The auxiliary regressions on revenue, domestic sales, and exports are useful. It might be worth moving at least one of these (perhaps revenue growth) into the main text to show that markup discipline coincides with lower sales.[^1]

### Interpretation and external validity

- Policy discussion: The conclusion suggests that trade liberalization and domestic antitrust policy can be partial substitutes for controlling market power. This is an interesting statement; it would benefit from being made more precise in terms of mechanisms (e.g. import competition disciplining high‑markup firms in concentrated sectors vs. antitrust acting on mergers/cartels) and perhaps citing a couple of related policy papers.[^1]
- Generalizability: The Turkish context features high dependence on imported intermediates and relatively concentrated manufacturing sectors. A short paragraph reflecting on how the results might translate (or not) to other emerging economies with different market structures or trade relationships would make the contribution more broadly relevant.[^1]

## Overall Assessment and Recommendation

This is a strong and ambitious M2 thesis that tackles an important question with a modern toolkit and an original combination of theory and empirics. The integration of an oligopoly model, a China‑shock design, selection correction, and sectoral decomposition is impressive for this level. The main empirical findings—that Chinese import competition disciplines markups of high‑markup incumbents in concentrated sectors while selection and entry margins are limited—are plausible and policy‑relevant.[^1]

Before submission, the thesis would benefit from tightening the theoretical exposition, sharpening the identification discussion, being slightly more cautious in interpreting the selection correction, and streamlining the empirical narrative around a small set of “headline” results. These revisions would improve clarity and credibility without requiring major new estimation work.[^1]

Conditional on addressing these points, the thesis is clearly of very good quality and demonstrates readiness for more advanced research work (e.g. PhD level) in trade and industrial organization.
