\textbf{Abstract}

\smallskip

This thesis estimates the effect of instrumented sectoral Chinese import-penetration growth on firm-level markup growth among observed incumbent manufacturers in Turkey. Guided by a parsimonious oligopoly model with variable markups and input-output linkages, I distinguish an output-competition channel from an input-supply channel using a shift-share design that combines 2009 Turkish exposure shares with changes in Chinese export performance in other high-income markets. The baseline IV estimates imply that stronger Chinese import penetration lowers markup growth for surviving firms, with larger effects in the upper tail of the markup-adjustment distribution and in more concentrated sectors. The input-supply shifter does not generate a robust within-firm markup effect, but it is associated with reallocation toward lower-markup firms. Sector-wide markup effects are weaker because reallocation partly offsets within-firm discipline. Because disappearance from ORBIS/AMADEUS is not equivalent to validated firm death, entry and exit results are interpreted as observed sample turnover rather than creative destruction in a strict sense.

\smallskip
\textit{Keywords}: Oligopoly, variable markups, shift-share instrument, import competition, firm dynamics, emerging markets

\section{Introduction}

Oligopoly is widespread in manufacturing, yet credible evidence on how foreign competition affects market power in oligopolistic environments remains limited, especially in emerging markets. This thesis asks how Chinese import competition affects markups in Turkish manufacturing once two complications are taken seriously: import exposure operates through both output-market competition and input costs, and firm-level outcomes are only observed for firms that remain in the sample. The central empirical estimand is therefore narrow by construction: the effect of instrumented sectoral Chinese import-penetration growth on firm-level markup growth among observed incumbent firms.

Turkey is a useful setting for this question. Since 2010, Turkish manufacturing has faced rising Chinese import penetration in sectors that are often concentrated and heavily reliant on imported intermediates. Chinese exposure can therefore compress markups through tougher product-market competition while also relaxing marginal costs through cheaper imported inputs.\footnote{\textcite{aghionOpposingFirmLevelResponses2024} emphasizes these opposing firm-level responses to Chinese import exposure.} Markups are the natural outcome for studying this tension because they connect trade shocks to prices, allocative efficiency, rent distribution, and industrial dynamics.

The empirical challenge is that these channels are easy to conflate. In models with variable markups, tougher import competition can reduce incumbents' pricing power.\footnote{Tougher import competition increases the elasticity of residual demand and compresses markups \parencite{melitzMarketSizeTrade2008, edmondCompetitionMarkupsGains2015}.} But observed average markups may also reflect selective survival, sample disappearance, and reallocation across firms \parencite{arkolakisElusiveProCompetitiveEffects2019}. In proprietary firm datasets such as ORBIS/AMADEUS, disappearance from the panel is not the same object as validated economic exit. A regression of markup changes on import penetration can therefore mix within-incumbent discipline with compositional change in the observed sample.

To separate these margins, I combine a parsimonious oligopoly model with a four-part empirical design. First, I instrument changes in Chinese import penetration with a shift-share IV that interacts 2009 Turkish sectoral exposure shares with Chinese export growth to other high-income destinations. Second, I construct an input-supply shifter from frozen Turkish input-output linkages to isolate the cost channel. Third, I use a flexible control-function correction based on lagged EBIT margin to assess whether selective sample survival materially changes the within-incumbent markup result. Fourth, I complement mean IV estimates with grouped IV quantile regressions and a sectoral decomposition into within-firm, reallocation, and observed entry/exit components.

This design yields three headline findings. First, stronger Chinese import penetration reduces markup growth among observed incumbent firms. Second, the negative effect is concentrated in the upper tail of the markup-adjustment distribution and is stronger in more concentrated sectors. Third, the input-supply channel does not produce a robust within-firm markup effect, but it does contribute to market-share reallocation toward lower-markup firms. At the sector level, total markup effects are weaker because reallocation partly offsets within-firm discipline. The selection correction leaves the baseline coefficient close to unchanged, so the main result is best interpreted as incumbent discipline in the observed panel rather than as evidence on creative destruction in a strict Schumpeterian sense.

The paper contributes to several literatures. First, it adds to quantitative trade models with oligopolistic competition and variable markups, where trade shocks affect pricing through residual demand, market-share movements, and pass-through \parencite{deblasUnderstandingMarkupsOpen2015,atkesonPricingtoMarketTradeCosts2008,gaubertGranularComparativeAdvantage2021}. The theoretical framework is intentionally parsimonious: its role is to organize sign predictions for the output-competition channel, the ambiguous markup effect of the input channel, and the decomposition between within-firm and reallocation margins.

Second, the thesis contributes to the empirical China-shock literature. Beginning with \autocite{autorChinaSyndromeLocal2013}, this literature uses shift-share designs to estimate the causal effects of Chinese import competition.\footnote{Much of this work focuses on labor markets, employment, and local outcomes \parencite{autorTradeAdjustmentWorkerLevel2014, acemogluImportCompetitionGreat2016,pierceSurprisinglySwiftDecline2016}, while other work studies innovation and productivity outcomes \parencite{aghionOpposingFirmLevelResponses2024, bloomTradeInducedTechnical2016}.} Related studies examine market power, prices, and concentration \parencite{deloeckerPricesMarkupsTrade2016,impullittiGlobalizationMarketPower2023,jaravelWhatArePrice,amitiUSMarketConcentration2025}. My contribution is to study markup dynamics directly at the firm level in an emerging economy while keeping output-competition and input-supply channels separate.

Third, the paper engages directly with the modern shift-share identification literature. The treatment varies at the sector-year level, identification comes from differences in predetermined Turkish exposure to common China-driven shocks, and the effective variation is concentrated in a limited set of influential sectors. This perspective follows the recent shift-share and Bartik literature, which emphasizes the roles of exposure-share exogeneity, shock-level identifying variation, and inference under correlated exposure structures \parencite{adaoShiftShareDesignsTheory2019,borusyakQuasiExperimentalShiftShareResearch2022,goldsmith-pinkhamBartikInstrumentsWhat2020}. I therefore interpret the estimates as local to the more exposed Turkish manufacturing sectors that carry substantial identifying weight.

Fourth, the thesis contributes to the literature on firm dynamics and distributional effects. The question is inherently dynamic because competition affects both firm behavior and survival \parencite{melitzImpactTradeIntraIndustry2003,ericsonMarkovPerfectIndustryDynamics1995,hopenhaynEntryExitFirm1992}. Turkey's decline in business dynamism makes this especially relevant \parencite{akcigitFactsBusinessDynamism2020}. The selection correction is not a structural exit model; rather, it is a reduced-form way to ask whether conditioning flexibly on lagged profitability materially changes the estimated within-incumbent response. The grouped-IV quantile analysis further shows that average effects conceal important heterogeneity across the markup-adjustment distribution \parencite{chetverikovIVQuantileRegression2016,galleSlicingPieQuantifying2023,bursteinBottomUpMarkupFluctuations2025,baqaeeProductivityMisallocationGeneral2020}.

The rest of the paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 presents the theoretical framework. Section 3 describes the data, variable construction, and sample definitions. Section 4 develops the identification strategy, selection correction, decomposition, and distributional design. Section 5 reports the main results. Section 6 presents robustness checks. Section 7 discusses mechanisms. Section 8 concludes. The appendix provides additional details on markup measurement, sample construction, and replication constraints.

\section{Theoretical framework}

\subsection{Model}
This section develops a tractable oligopoly model with variable markups, following \textcite{atkesonPricingtoMarketTradeCosts2008}, which links Chinese import shocks to firm-level markups, market shares, and sectoral market power in Turkish manufacturing. Here I drop time $t$ for simplicity. 

\textbf{Environment}: Time is discrete, indexed by $t=0,1,2,...$. The economy contains a continuum of manufacturing sectors indexed by $j \in [0,1]$. In each sector, there is a finite set of domestic firms $i = 1, ..., N_j$ that produce differentiated varieties and compete in quantities à la Cournot. The number of active domestic firms is endogenous because firms may exit after observing current profitability and the continuation value of staying active. 

Domestic firms sell differentiated varieties in the domestic market and compete strategically. Foreign producers affect the domestic equilibrium through two distinct channels. First, through the output-competition channel, imported final goods compete directly with domestic production in the domestic product market, which affects the residual demand faced by domestic firms and therefore their optimal markups. Second, through the input-supply channel, imported intermediate inputs affect domestic firms' marginal costs through direct and indirect sourcing linkages, which affects production costs and therefore equilibrium prices, quantities, shares, and exit decisions. Let $\tau^O_j, \tau^I_j$ denote the output-competition and input-supply states in sector $j$, respectively. In the dynamic setting, each active firm $i$ is characterized by the state vector $x_{ijt}$ that includes its productivity, demand shifters, lagged market share, the import shocks $\tau^O_{jt}, \tau^I_{jt}$, and other relevant state variables.

\textbf{Final demand}: A representative household supplies inelastic labor and consumes a final aggregate $C$ constructed from sectoral composites $Q_j$: 
\[C = \Bigg(\int_{0}^{1} \alpha_j^{\frac{1}{\eta}} Q_{jt}^{\frac{\eta-1}{\eta}}dj \Bigg)^{\frac{\eta}{\eta-1}}, \quad \eta > 1, \int_{0}^{1} \alpha_j dj = 1\]
where $\alpha_j$ is the preference weight for sector $j$ and $\eta$ is the elasticity of substitution between sectors. Let $P_{j}$ denote the sectoral price index and $P$ the consumer CES price index. Standard aggregation implies $P = \bigg(\int_{0}^{1} \alpha_j P_{jt}^{1-\eta} dj \bigg)^{\frac{1}{1-\eta}}$. Given total nominal expenditure $Y_t$, sectoral expenditure (domestic absorption) in sector $j$ is given by $D_{j} = \alpha_j \bigg(\frac{P_j}{P}\bigg)^{1-\eta} Y_t$. 

\textbf{Demand side for domestic firms}: Within each sector, domestic absorption $D_{jt}$ is split between domestic and foreign suppliers (China plus the rest of the world). Let $\Lambda_j^D \in (0,1]$ denote the CES weight on the domestic composite in sector $j$, so that the foreign weight is $1-\Lambda_j^D$. Use a nested CES representation:
$$Q_{jt} = \bigg[ (\Lambda_j^D)^{\frac{1}{\nu_j}} (Q_{jt}^D)^{\frac{\nu_j-1}{\nu_j}} + (1-\Lambda_j^D)^{\frac{1}{\nu_j}} (Q_{jt}^F)^{\frac{\nu_j-1}{\nu_j}} \bigg]^{\frac{\nu_j}{\nu_j-1}}$$

where $Q_{jt}^D$ and $Q_{jt}^F$ are the domestic and foreign composites, respectively, and $\nu_j > 1$ is the elasticity of substitution between domestic and foreign varieties. For a given change in domestic prices, a higher foreign weight means stronger substitution towards foreign varieties, which increases the elasticity of residual demand faced by domestic firms. Assume $\rho_j > \nu_j > \eta$, so that the elasticity of substitution between domestic varieties is higher than the elasticity between domestic and foreign varieties, which is in turn higher than the elasticity between sectors. This assumption implies that the pro-competitive effect of import competition is stronger for firms with higher market shares and in more concentrated sectors.

Within the domestic segment of sector $j$, consumers have CES preferences over the differentiated varieties supplied by domestic firms $i = 1, ..., N_j$:
\[Q_{j}^D = \Bigg(\sum_{i=1}^{N_j} \xi_i^{\frac{1}{\rho_j}} q_{ij}^{\frac{\rho_j-1}{\rho_j}} \Bigg)^{\frac{\rho_j}{\rho_j-1}}, \quad \rho_j > 1\]

where $\rho_j$ is the elasticity of substitution between domestic varieties, $\xi_i$ is the variety-specific share parameter, and $q_{ij}$ is the quantity of variety $i$ in sector $j$. The associated domestic price index is $P_j^D = \bigg(\sum_{i=1}^{N_j} \xi_i p_{ij}^{1-\rho_j} \bigg)^{\frac{1}{1-\rho_j}}$. Conditional on $Q_j^D$, the demand facing firm $i$ is $q_{ij} = \xi_i \bigg(\frac{p_{ij}}{P_j^D}\bigg)^{-\rho_j} Q_j^D$. Then, we can define the within-domestic sales share of firm $i$ as $\tilde{s}_{ij} = \frac{p_{ij} q_{ij}}{P_{j}^D Q_{j}^D}$.

\textbf{Production and costs}: Each domestic firm produces using labor and an intermediate input bundle according to a Cobb-Douglas technology: $Y_{ij} = \varphi_{ij} L_{ij}^{\beta_j} M_{ij}^{1-\beta_j}$, with $0 < \beta_j < 1$, where $\varphi_{ij}$ is firm productivity, $L_{ij}$ is labor input, and $M_{ij}$ is the intermediate input bundle, and $\beta_j$ is the labor share in production. Let $w$ denote the wage and $P^M_j$ the price index of the intermediate-input bundle. Under cost minimization with competitive factor markets, the exact nominal marginal cost of firm $i$ in sector $j$ is
\[
\text{mc}_{ij}
=
\frac{1}{\varphi_{ij}}
\left( \frac{w}{\beta_j} \right)^{\beta_j}
\left( \frac{P^M_j}{1-\beta_j} \right)^{1-\beta_j},
\]
so, up to the sector-specific constant $\beta_j^{-\beta_j}(1-\beta_j)^{-(1-\beta_j)}$, the simpler proportionality used below is $\text{mc}_{ij} \propto \varphi_{ij}^{-1} w^{\beta_j} (P^M_j)^{1-\beta_j}$.

Taking logs and differentiating yields the change in log marginal cost:
\[\Delta \ln \text{mc}_{ij} = -\Delta \ln \varphi_{ij} + \beta_j \Delta \ln w + (1-\beta_j) \Delta \ln P^M_j\]
The intermediate bundle $M_{ij}$ is a composite of sectoral input bundles indexed by $k$, and each sectoral input price is itself a composite of domestic and Chinese sourcing shares. From CES aggregation and log-linearization, the log change in the intermediate-input price in sector $j$ can be written as $d \ln P^M_{j} \approx \sum_k l_{jk} \omega_k^{CHN} d \ln P^{CHN}_{k}$, where $l_{jk}$ is the $(j,k)$-element of the Leontief inverse of the input-output matrix, and $\omega_k^{CHN}$ is the Chinese sourcing weight. Thus, changes in Chinese input prices can feed into domestic marginal costs through network propagation and sourcing intensity. Substituting this approximation into the marginal cost equation gives: 
\begin{equation}
d \ln \text{mc}_{ij} \approx -d \ln \varphi_{ij} + \beta_j d \ln w + (1-\beta_j) \sum_k l_{jk} \omega_k^{CHN} d \ln P^{CHN}_{k}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Oligopoly and markups}: In each sector $j$, domestic firms compete à la Cournot, taking their marginal costs and the residual demand curve as given. Firm $i$'s profit is $\pi_{ij} = (p_{ij} - \text{mc}_{ij}) q_{ij} - f_{ij}$, where $f_{ij}$ is a period fixed cost. Because demand is nested CES, a firm's perceived own-price elasticity is endogenous and depends on within-domestic, domestic-foreign, and between-sector substitution patterns. Let $s_{ij}$ denote the share of firm $i$ in total sectoral sales, so that $s_{ij} = \Lambda^D_{j} \tilde{s}_{ij}$. Under Cournot competition, the perceived elasticity faced by firm $i$ is 
\[ \sigma^C_{ij} = \Bigg(\frac{1-\tilde{s}_{ij}}{\rho_j} + \frac{\tilde{s}_{ij} - s_{ij}}{\nu_j} + \frac{{s}_{ij}}{\eta} \Bigg)^{-1} \]
where $\nu_j$ is the elasticity between domestic and foreign composites within the sector. The firm's optimal markup is therefore $\mu_{ij} \equiv \frac{p_{ij}}{\text{mc}_{ij}} = \frac{\sigma^C_{ij}}{\sigma^C_{ij} - 1}$. This formulation implies that perceived demand elasticity is decreasing in firm size, while markups are increasing in market share. The Cournot markup condition is therefore easiest to write in inverse-markup form:
\begin{equation}
\frac{1}{\mu_{ij}} = \Bigg( 1- \frac{1}{\rho_j}\Bigg) - \Bigg[\frac{1}{\Lambda^D_{j}} \Bigg(\frac{1}{\nu_j} - \frac{1}{\rho_j}\Bigg) + \Bigg(\frac{1}{\eta} - \frac{1}{\nu_j} \Bigg) \Bigg] {s}_{ij}
\end{equation}
Equivalently, the markup itself is
\begin{equation}
\mu_{ij} = \frac{1}{\Big( 1- \frac{1}{\rho_j}\Big) - \Big[\frac{1}{\Lambda^D_{j}} \Big(\frac{1}{\nu_j} - \frac{1}{\rho_j}\Big) + \Big(\frac{1}{\eta} - \frac{1}{\nu_j} \Big) \Big] {s}_{ij}}
\end{equation}
Define the corresponding Herfindahl index as $\text{HHI}_j = \sum_i^{N_j} s^2_{ij}$ and define the sectoral average markup as the harmonic mean of firm markups weighted by market shares, $\mu_j = \bigg(\sum_i^{N_j} \frac{s_{ij}}{\mu_{ij}}\bigg)^{-1}$. Then the sectoral inverse markup can be expressed as a function of the HHI and the parameters of the model:
\begin{equation}
\frac{1}{\mu_j} = \Bigg( 1- \frac{1}{\rho_j}\Bigg) - \Bigg[\frac{1}{\Lambda^D_{j}} \Bigg( \frac{1}{\nu_j} - \frac{1}{\rho_j}\Bigg) + \Bigg(\frac{1}{\eta} - \frac{1}{\nu_j} \Bigg) \Bigg] \text{HHI}_j
\end{equation}
so that
\begin{equation}
\mu_j = \frac{1}{\Big( 1- \frac{1}{\rho_j}\Big) - \Big[\frac{1}{\Lambda^D_{j}} \Big( \frac{1}{\nu_j} - \frac{1}{\rho_j}\Big) + \Big(\frac{1}{\eta} - \frac{1}{\nu_j} \Big) \Big] \text{HHI}_j}
\end{equation}
Sectoral markups are therefore increasing in concentration. Moreover, the slope of the concentration-markup relationship depends on the domestic expenditure share $\Lambda^D_{j}$, which means stronger foreign competition alters the perceived elasticity relevant for high-share firms and therefore changes the markup responses to concentration.

\textbf{Dynamic extension with endogenous exit}: A firm that remains active in period $t$ receives current profits and retains the option value of future operation. Let $V(x_{ijt})$ denote the value of continuing. The firm's Bellman equation is: $$V(x_{ijt}) = \max \{ 0, \pi(x_{ijt}) + \beta \mathbb{E} [V(x_{ij,t+1}) \mid x_{ijt}] \}$$ where $\beta \in (0,1)$ is the discount factor. Exit yields normalized continuation value zero. The optimal exit rule is: $Exit_{ijt}=1 \Leftrightarrow \pi(x_{ijt}) + \beta \mathbb{E} [V(x_{ij,t+1}) \mid x_{ijt}] < 0 $. Equivalently, there exists a threshold function $\theta^*(x_{ijt})$ such that a firms exits if its latent profitability index lies below the cutoff: $\theta(x_{ijt}) < \theta^*(x_{ijt})$. 

This shows that trade shocks affect both current profitability and the continuation value, so any observed firm-level outcome among survivors is selected on unobserved state variables. Hence, the effects of trade shocks on survival is theoretically ambiguous. On the one hand, tougher import competition can reduce current profits for weak firms, tending to raise exit. On the other hand, firms that benefit from input-cost reductions can remain sufficiently profitable to stay active. Consequently, the model allows strong incumbent discipline to coexist with modest aggregate selection, and motivates the empirical strategy to separate these effects.

\textbf{Equilibrium}: Given sectoral demand shifters, input prices, and technology, a static sectoral equilibrium is a collection of prices, quantities, and markups, such that the households allocate expenditure across sectors and domestic varieties, firms choose outputs to maximize profit under Cournot competition, and goods markets clear. The equilibrium is characterized by the system of equations described above, which can be solved numerically given parameter values and functional forms. The model delivers predictions on how changes in Chinese import penetration, through both output competition and input supply channels, affect firm-level markups, market shares, and sectoral markups.

\subsection{Testable predictions}

The model yields the following testable implications.
\paragraph{Prediction 1.} Stronger Chinese import penetration in final goods lowers firm-level markups on average.  
A rise in foreign competition reduces the domestic expenditure share $\Lambda_j^D$ and raises the residual demand elasticity faced by domestic incumbents. From equation (2), this lowers the optimal markup of the representative domestic firm. Thus, holding technology and input costs fixed: $\frac{\partial \mu_{ij}}{\partial \tau_j^O} < 0$

\paragraph{Prediction 2.} The disciplining effect of output competition is stronger for large firms and in concentrated sectors.  
Because the perceived elasticity under Cournot competition depends on firm market share and sector concentration, the same increase in foreign competitive pressure compresses markups more strongly for firms with high initial market shares and in sectors with higher $HHI_j$. In this model, large firms face a larger change in residual demand elasticity when foreign competition intensifies. Hence,
\[
\frac{\partial^2 \mu_{ij}}{\partial \tau_j^O \partial s_{ij}} < 0
\qquad \text{and} \qquad
\frac{\partial^2 \mu_{j}}{\partial \tau_j^O \partial HHI_j} < 0,
\]
under the maintained parameter restrictions $\rho_j > \nu_j > \eta$.

\paragraph{Prediction 3.} Input-supply shocks operate primarily through marginal costs and market shares. A favorable Chinese input shock lowers the cost of intermediate inputs in proportion to sectoral input-output exposure. From equation (1): $\frac{\partial \ln mc_{ij}}{\partial \tau_j^I} < 0$ for firms in sectors with stronger exposure to Chinese intermediate inputs, where $\tau_j^I$ denotes the input-supply shock. Lower marginal costs improve firms' competitive position and tend to expand their equilibrium market shares, so that $\frac{\partial s_{ij}}{\partial \tau_j^I} > 0$ for sufficiently exposed firms. By contrast, the effect on within-firm markups is in general ambiguous: $\frac{\partial \mu_{ij}}{\partial \tau_j^I} \gtrless 0$. The reason is that lower marginal cost may raise markups mechanically, but it also induces equilibrium price adjustment and stronger competitive responses. Accordingly, the input-supply channel is expected to matter primarily for market-share reallocation rather than for average within-firm markup compression.

\paragraph{Prediction 4.} Sectoral markup changes need not mirror average firm-level markup changes. Even if output competition lowers many firms' markups, the aggregate sector response may be attenuated or amplified depending on whether market shares shift toward low-markup or high-markup firms. Thus, the effect of trade shocks on $\mu_j$ is in general a combination of within-firm discipline and between-firm reallocation.

\paragraph{Prediction 5.} Observed firm-level markup changes may then combine a within-incumbent response with a compositional effect induced by endogenous exit. This motivates the selection-correction strategy developed in Section 4.2.

\section{Data and Measurement}
In this section, I describe the data sources, define the samples and key variables I use in the analysis, and present summary statistics.

\subsection{Data sources}
\textbf{Firm-level data.} The ORBIS/AMADEUS dataset contains harmonized balance-sheet, income-statement, and profit-and-loss information for firms. I use the Turkish manufacturing panel from 2009 to 2023 and map firms from NACE Rev.2 into 4-digit ISIC sectors using a concordance. The raw manufacturing panel contains 15,957 firms and 78,921 firm-year observations. Because the main dependent variable is markup growth, the estimation sample is necessarily smaller than the raw panel: a firm-year enters the baseline markup regressions only when current and lagged accounting variables are both observed and revenue, COGS, and capital are positive. After applying these restrictions, winsorizing extreme variables at the 1st and 99th percentiles, and adding lagged controls, the main firm-level specifications use about 31,000 firm-year observations.

The panel is unbalanced, and I do not impute intermittent reporting gaps. I define \emph{observed exit} as disappearance from ORBIS/AMADEUS between $t-1$ and $t$. This operational definition is useful for studying sample composition, but it is not equivalent to validated legal or economic firm death. Throughout the thesis, results on entry and exit should therefore be read as evidence on observed sample turnover unless otherwise stated.

\textbf{Trade data.} My main treatment variable is Chinese import penetration at the 4-digit ISIC sector-year level, defined as
\begin{equation}
    \begin{aligned}
    \text{China IP}_{jt} = \frac{\text{Import}_{jt}^{\text{CHN} \to \text{TR}}}{\text{Output}_{jt} + \text{Import}_{jt}^{\text{World} \to \text{TR}} - \text{Export}_{jt}^{\text{TR} \to \text{World}}} 
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where the denominator corresponds to domestic absorption. I construct the numerator from CEPII BACI HS96 bilateral trade data at the HS 6-digit level and map products to 4-digit ISIC sectors using the HS-ISIC concordance of Liao et al. (2021). Sectoral output and trade aggregates used in the denominator come from UNIDO ISDB4/INDSTAT4. The treatment in the regressions is the annual change in this import-penetration rate.

For the input-supply channel, I propagate the output-side shift-share shock through Turkish input-output linkages. The weights come from the 2003 Turkish input-output table in WIOD and are kept fixed over the sample period. Using frozen weights is a deliberate trade-off: it cannot capture subsequent changes in sourcing networks, but it preserves a predetermined exposure matrix and avoids loading the upstream shifter with contemporaneous endogenous restructuring.

\textbf{Other data.} I use several TURKSTAT datasets to construct controls and deflators. The 3-digit Producer Price Index (PPI) is used to deflate nominal variables. Personnel-cost and value-added data from the Annual Industry and Service Statistics are used to construct pre-period sectoral labor intensity. Across the empirical sections, the firm-level outcome data are therefore combined with a sector-year treatment design and a smaller set of effective identifying cells.

\subsection{Chinese import competition}

Estimating the effect of changes in Chinese import penetration on firm outcomes by OLS is problematic for at least three reasons. First, Turkish sectoral demand or productivity shocks may affect both imports from China and domestic firm outcomes. Second, persistent sectoral characteristics may jointly shape exposure to Chinese competition and firm performance. Third, the treatment varies at the sector-year level while the main outcomes are observed at the firm-year level, so naive firm-level regressions risk overstating the amount of independent identifying variation.

To address these issues, I implement a shift-share instrumental-variable design following \textcite{autorTradeAdjustmentWorkerLevel2014} and the shock-level interpretation in \textcite{borusyakQuasiExperimentalShiftShareResearch2022}. The instrument combines exogenous shifts in Chinese export performance in other high-income markets with predetermined Turkish exposure shares. In the baseline, the exposure shares are fixed at $t_0 = 2009$. This construction is meant to isolate variation in Turkish import competition that comes from common China-driven shocks rather than from contemporaneous Turkish sectoral conditions.

Formally, let $M_{jt}^{\text{CHN} \to \text{HI}}$ denote Chinese exports to high-income countries in sector $j$ at time $t$, and let $M_{jt}^{\text{World} \to \text{HI}}$ denote total world exports to high-income countries in the same sector and period. The shift component of the instrument is defined as the change in China’s export share in high-income destination markets:
\[
g_{jt} = \Delta \ln \text{Import Share}_{jt}^{\text{CHN} \to \text{HI}} = \Delta \ln M_{jt}^{\text{CHN} \to \text{HI}} - \Delta \ln M_{jt}^{\text{World} \to \text{HI}}
\]
The share component is the baseline level of Chinese import penetration in Turkey in period $t_0 = 2009$. The shift-share instrument for changes in Chinese import penetration is therefore
\[
Z_{jt}^{\text{Output}} = s_{j,\text{CHN}}^{2009} \times g_{jt}.
\]
To capture the input-supply channel separately, I construct an additional control variable by weighting the sector-level output instrument by Turkish input-output linkages across sectors, following \textcite{amitiTradeLiberalizationIntermediate2007}:
\[
Z_{jt}^{\text{Input}} = \sum_k w_{jk}^{t_0} \times Z_{kt}^{\text{Output}}
\]
where $w_{jk}^{t_0}$ denotes the technical coefficient derived from the 2003 Turkish input-output table, defined as the share of sector $k$ in total intermediate inputs used by sector $j$:
\[
w_{jk}^{t_0} = \frac{\text{Domestic Use}_{jk} + \text{Imported Use}_{jk}}{\text{Total Intermediate Inputs}_j}
\]

The relevant treatment cell in all IV specifications is therefore the sector-year, not the firm-year. In the baseline sample, the firm-level regressions reuse 609 effective sector-year cells spanning 95 ISIC4 manufacturing sectors. Sector fixed effects absorb time-invariant sector heterogeneity, year fixed effects absorb aggregate shocks common across sectors, and placebo-lead tests assess whether future China shocks predict current outcomes. The main remaining threat is a sector-specific global demand or technology shock that simultaneously raises Chinese exports to other high-income markets and changes Turkish markups. The identifying assumption is that, conditional on fixed effects and controls, these residual sector-year shocks are orthogonal to the constructed shift. Because Rotemberg-weight diagnostics show that some sectors are much more influential than others, I interpret the IV estimates as local to the more exposed sector-year cells rather than as universal effects for all Turkish manufacturing sectors.


\subsection{Markup estimation and other variables}

\textbf{Markups.} I estimate markups using the production approach pioneered by \textcite{hallRelationPriceMarginal1988} and developed by \textcite{deloeckerMarkupsFirmLevelExport2012}. In the baseline implementation, I estimate a Cobb-Douglas revenue production function within ISIC4 sectors, use COGS as the flexible input, and recover the output elasticity of COGS from that production function. The markup estimator is
\[
\mu_{it} = \theta_{ivt} \frac{P_{it}Q_{it}}{P_{vit}V_{it}},
\]
where $\theta_{ivt}$ is the output elasticity of the flexible input, $P_{it}Q_{it}$ is firm revenue, and $P_{vit}V_{it}$ is expenditure on input $v$. COGS is the most consistently observed short-run variable input in ORBIS/AMADEUS and is therefore the baseline flexible input. Appendix A.1 reports the implementation details, a translog sensitivity check, and the Kirov et al. robustness exercise.

Because accounting-based markup levels are sensitive to production-function assumptions, reporting quality, and the choice of flexible input, I focus on within-firm changes rather than levels \parencite{deridderHitchhikersGuideMarkup2026}. The main dependent variable is
\[
\Delta \log \hat{\mu}_{it} \equiv \log \hat{\mu}_{it} - \log \hat{\mu}_{i,t-1}
\]
This choice is meant to limit the influence of time-invariant reporting differences and level mismeasurement.

\textbf{Labor Intensity.} Labor intensity is constructed as the ratio of personnel costs to value added factor cost, averaged over 2009--2015 at the 4-digit sector level. This time-invariant measure captures differences in production technology across industries. The interaction of labor intensity with a post-2016 dummy captures differential effects after the minimum wage hike, which may have caused attrition of formal firms from the ORBIS database.

\textbf{Sectoral Markup.} Following \textcite{bursteinBottomUpMarkupFluctuations2025}, sectoral markup is defined as the market-share-weighted harmonic mean of firm markups:
\[
\mu_{jt} = \left( \sum_{i \in j} s_{ijt} \mu_{it}^{-1} \right)^{-1}
\]
where $s_{ijt} = \frac{D_{it}}{\sum_{i' \in j} D_{i't}}$ is firm market share, which is firm domestic sales deflated by industry PPI. The harmonic mean is appropriate because it gives more weight to low-markup firms, which are more likely to be affected by import competition and selection. The change in sectoral markup is then defined as the first difference of the log of the sectoral markup.

\section{Empirical strategy}
The empirical strategy has four components. First, I estimate a baseline shift-share IV specification for firm-level markup growth while controlling for the input-supply channel. Second, I add a reduced-form selection correction to ask whether the within-incumbent estimate is materially affected by selective survival in the observed panel. Third, I aggregate firm outcomes to the sector-year level and decompose sectoral markup changes into within-firm, reallocation, and observed entry/exit terms. Fourth, I examine heterogeneity across the distribution of markup adjustments using grouped IV quantile regressions.

\subsection{Identification strategy and baseline IV regression}

My baseline IV specification estimates the effect of changes in Chinese import penetration on firm-level markup adjustments, controlling for the input-supply channel and other covariates. The regression is specified as follows:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
Y_{ijt} &= \beta_O \Delta IP_{jt} + \beta_I Z_{jt}^{\text{Input}} + \gamma_1 \text{LaborShare}_{jt_0} \times \text{Post2016}_t \\
&\quad + \gamma_2 \text{LaborShare}_{jt_0} + X'_{ijt-1} \gamma + \tau_t + \delta_j + \varepsilon_{ijt}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

where $Y_{ijt}$ is the outcome of interest (observed exit probability or markup adjustments), $\Delta IP_{jt}$ is the change in Chinese import penetration, $Z_{jt}^{\text{Input}}$ is the control variable for the input-supply channel, $\text{LaborShare}_{jt_0}$ is the pre-2016 averaged labor intensity, $X_{ijt-1}$ are lagged firm-level controls, and $\tau_t$ and $\delta_j$ are year and sector fixed effects. The coefficient of interest is $\beta_O$, which measures the effect of output-market import competition after controlling for upstream input shocks and observables.

Identification comes from the output-side shift-share IV $Z_{jt}^{\text{Output}}$ for $\Delta IP_{jt}$, while $Z_{jt}^{\text{Input}}$ remains included as a control. Two clarifications are important. First, the treatment cell is the sector-year and the baseline exposure share is fixed in 2009, so the firm-level regression does not create additional independent shocks beyond those sector-year cells. Second, the coefficient should be interpreted as local to the sector-year cells that receive the most identifying weight. The Rotemberg-weight robustness reported later shows that a limited set of sectors is particularly influential, which disciplines the external-validity claim of the baseline estimate.

The control for pre-2016 labor share interacted with the post-2016 dummy captures differential exposure to the minimum-wage episode, which may have affected the formal-firm coverage of ORBIS \parencite{akcigitFactsBusinessDynamism2020}. Because firms with similar exposure structures may have correlated residuals, inference is clustered at the ISIC4 level and complemented, where available, with wild-cluster bootstrap p-values in the robustness section.

To assess the plausibility of the exclusion restriction, I conduct lead-placebo tests. If the instrument is valid, future realizations of the China shock should not predict current outcomes once fixed effects and controls are included. Conversely, significant placebo coefficients would suggest pre-trends or omitted sectoral shocks that threaten the design.

Formally, for horizon $k \in \{1,2,3\}$, I estimate:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
Y_{ijt} &= \beta^{k}_O Z^{\text{Output}}_{j,t+k} + \beta_I0 Z_{jt}^{\text{Input}} + \beta_{I1} Z_{j,t-1}^{\text{Input}} + \gamma_1 \text{LaborShare}_{jt_0} \times \text{Post2016}_t \\
&\quad + \gamma_2 \text{LaborShare}_{jt_0} + X'_{ijt-1} \gamma + \tau_t + \delta_j + \varepsilon_{ijt}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Under the identifying assumption, the placebo coefficients $\beta^{k}_O$ should be statistically indistinguishable from zero, providing evidence against pre-trends and supporting the validity of the instrument. I summarize these placebo coefficients graphically using a coefficient plot with confidence intervals across horizons.

\subsection{Selection correction}

A key concern in the baseline regression is endogenous sample selection. Firm-level markup adjustments are observed only for firms that remain in the panel and continue reporting. In ORBIS/AMADEUS, disappearance may reflect true economic exit, reporting gaps, or coverage changes. The empirical problem is the same in each case: if Chinese import competition affects observed survival and that margin is correlated with unobserved determinants of markup adjustment, then the baseline IV estimates mix a within-firm response with compositional change in the observed sample.

To address this issue, I adopt a semiparametric control-function correction inspired by the dynamic firm literature of \textcite{ericsonMarkovPerfectIndustryDynamics1995} and the reduced-form selection approach of \textcite{backusWhyProductivityCorrelated2020}. The objective is not to estimate a structural exit rule. Rather, it is to purge the markup equation of selection on unobservables: if observed survival depends on latent firm state and that state also affects markup adjustment, then the regression error among observed firms is not mean-independent of trade exposure.

I proxy the latent state with lagged EBIT margin, denoted $s_{i,t-1}$. This variable is observable, persistent, predictive of observed exit, and informative about subsequent markup adjustment, so it provides a practical control-function index for selection. Let the second-stage error decompose as $\varepsilon_{i(j)t} = \lambda_{jt} + \eta_{it}$, where $\lambda_{jt}$ captures sector-year shocks and $\eta_{it}$ is an idiosyncratic component. Among surviving firms, I model the selection-induced component as a flexible function of the lagged state proxy,
\[
\mathbb{E}[\eta_{it} \mid s_{it-1}] = g(s_{it-1}),
\]
and approximate $g(\cdot)$ with a third-order polynomial in $s_{it-1}$. The cubic is flexible enough to capture nonlinear selection on profitability without overfitting a relatively small support of the state proxy. The corrected second-stage regression therefore includes this control function:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\Delta \ln \mu_{i(j)t} &= \beta_O \widehat{\Delta IP_{jt}} + \beta_I Z_{jt}^{\text{Input}} + \gamma_1 \text{LaborShare}_{jt_0} \times \text{Post2016}_t \\
&\quad + \gamma_2 \text{LaborShare}_{jt_0} + X'_{ijt-1} \gamma + \tau_t + \delta_j + g(s_{it-1}) + \epsilon_{i(j)t}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Conditional on the flexible control function $g(s_{it-1})$, the remaining variation in the instrumented import penetration shock identifies the within-incumbent effect on markup adjustments, net of selection bias under this reduced-form approximation. Thus, $\beta_O$ should be interpreted as a within-incumbent treatment effect among observed survivors rather than as a reduced-form combination of treatment and composition.

\subsection{Decomposition of sectoral markup changes}

Firm-level regressions identify whether Chinese import penetration disciplines incumbents' pricing behavior, but they do not by themselves show how these responses aggregate to the sector level. This matters because sectoral markups may move differently from firm-level markups when import shocks also reallocate market shares across incumbents or change which firms are observed in adjacent years. To connect micro responses to sector-wide outcomes, I therefore decompose annual changes in sectoral markups into within-firm, reallocation, observed entry, and observed exit components. This decomposition is suited to environments with variable markups, where sectoral markup movements depend jointly on firm-specific markup adjustment and changes in market shares. 

Let $S_t$ denote the set of firms observed in sector $j$ at time $t$, $C = S_{t-1} \cap S_t$ the set of continuing firms, $E = S_t \setminus S_{t-1}$ observed entrants, and $X = S_{t-1} \setminus S_t$ observed exiters. Adapted from \textcite{melitzDynamicOlleyPakesProductivity2015}, the change in sectoral inverse markup can then be written as:
\[\Delta \mu_{jt}^{-1}
=
\underbrace{\sum_{i \in C} \bar{s}_{ij}\,\Delta \mu_{ij}^{-1}}_{\text{Within (continuers)}}
+
\underbrace{\sum_{i \in C} \Delta s_{ij}\,\bar{\mu}_{ij}^{-1}}_{\text{Between (continuers)}}
+
\underbrace{\sum_{i \in E} s_{ijt}\,\mu_{ijt}^{-1}}_{\text{Observed entry}} - \underbrace{\sum_{i \in X} s_{ij,t-1}\,\mu_{ij,t-1}^{-1}}_{\text{Observed exit}}\]
where $\bar{s}_{ij} = \frac{1}{2}(s_{ijt} + s_{ijt-1})$ and $\bar{\mu}_{ij}^{-1} = \frac{1}{2}(\mu_{it}^{-1} + \mu_{it-1}^{-1})$ are the average market share and inverse markup for firm $i$ over the two periods. This decomposition allows me to isolate the contributions of different mechanisms to sectoral markup changes.

The within term captures markup adjustment among continuing firms, holding their market shares fixed at midpoint weights. It therefore measures the contribution of incumbent pricing responses to aggregate markup discipline. The reallocation term captures changes in sectoral inverse markups due to shifts in market shares across continuing firms, holding their markups fixed at midpoint values. This term is positive when market shares move toward firms with lower markups and negative when reallocation favors firms with higher markups. The observed-entry term measures the contribution of firms newly appearing in year $t$, while the observed-exit term captures the contribution of firms present in $t-1$ that disappear in $t$. Because the decomposition is written in inverse markups, a positive component corresponds to a decline in sectoral markups, that is, a pro-competitive effect, while a negative component corresponds to an increase in sectoral markups, that is, an anti-competitive effect.

I compute these four components for each sector-year and then use them separately as dependent variables in the sector-level IV specifications. This allows the import-competition shock to affect aggregate markups through distinct channels. Formally, for each outcome $m \in \{ \text{within-firm, reallocation, observed entry, observed exit} \}$, I estimate: 
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    Y^m_{jt} &= \beta_O^m \widehat{\Delta IP}_{jt} + \beta_I^m Z_{jt}^{\text{Input}} + \Gamma' W_{jt} + \delta_j + \tau_t + \epsilon_{jt}^m
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $Y^m_{jt}$ is the sector-level outcome corresponding to component $m$, $\widehat{\Delta IP_{jt}}$ is the change in Chinese import penetration instrumented by the output-competition shock, $Z_{jt}^{\text{Input}}$ is the control for input supply channel, $W_{jt}$ are sector-level controls, and $\delta_j$ and $\tau_t$ are sector and year fixed effects. The coefficient $\beta_O^m$ captures the effect of import competition on the specific channel $m$.

This decomposition is central because the aggregate response of sectoral markups need not mirror the average firm-level response. Even if import competition disciplines continuing firms, the sectoral effect may be attenuated or reversed when market shares are reallocated toward dominant incumbents, or when observed entry and exit contribute in offsetting directions. Estimating the four terms separately therefore makes it possible to distinguish whether sectoral markup changes are primarily driven by incumbent adjustments, reallocation of market shares, or observed sample turnover. In addition, to assess the relative importance of each margin, I express each decomposition component as a share of the total absolute change in inverse sector markup. 

\subsection{Distributional effects and concentration heterogeneity}

To move beyond average effects, I examine whether the impact of import competition varies across the distribution of firm-level markup adjustments. This is important because mean IV estimates may mask whether trade discipline is concentrated among firms at the upper tail of the markup-adjustment distribution, where pricing power is likely to be strongest. I therefore complement the baseline specification with a grouped IV quantile approach \textcite{chetverikovIVQuantileRegression2016} adapted to the settings in which the endogenous treatment varies at the sector-year level. 

I define $\rho_{jt}^{(k)}$ as the $k$-th decile of the distribution of the within-sector-year distribution of firm-level markup adjustments among survivors. For each decile $k$, the regression is specified as:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\rho_{jt}^{(k)} &= \beta_O^{(k)} \widehat{\Delta IP_{jt}} + \beta_I^{(k)} Z_{jt}^{\text{Input}} + \gamma_1^{(k)} \text{LaborShare}_{jt_0} \times \text{Post2016}_t \\
&\quad + \gamma_2^{(k)} \text{LaborShare}_{jt_0} + X'_{jt-1} \gamma^{(k)} + \tau_t^{(k)} + \delta_j^{(k)} + \nu_{jt}^{(k)}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

The grouped formulation is preferable to a standard firm-level IV quantile regression because the treatment is common within sector-year cells. Pooling firms directly would require rank-similarity assumptions across sectors that are difficult to justify in the presence of sector-level unobservables. By using sector-year quantiles as the dependent variable, the specification instead isolates how import competition shifts different parts of the outcome distribution across groups. 

Identification relies on the same shift-share design as in the baseline analysis. Sectoral changes in Chinese import penetration are instrumented using exogenous growth in Chinese exports to high-income destinations interacted with predetermined Turkish exposure shares, while controlling for the input supply channel. Conditional on fixed effects and controls, $\widehat{\Delta IP_{jt}}$ is therefore interpreted as exogenous variation in output-market competitive pressure. 

Because sample quantiles are mechanically sensitive to the number of firms in each sector-year cell, particularly in smaller sectors, I correct for order-statistic bias (Neyman and Scott, 1948) by augmenting the specification with a flexible control function of the number of firm observations $n_{jt}$:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\rho_{jt}^{(k)} &= \beta_O^{(k)} \widehat{\Delta IP_{jt}} + \beta_I^{(k)} Z_{jt}^{\text{Input}} + \gamma_1^{(k)} \text{LaborShare}_{jt_0} \times \text{Post2016}_t \\
&\quad + \gamma_2^{(k)} \text{LaborShare}_{jt_0} + X'_{jt-1} \gamma^{(k)} + \tau_t^{(k)} + \delta_j^{(k)} + g^{(k)}(n_{jt}) + \nu_{jt}^{(k)}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $g^{(k)}(\cdot)$ is approximated with a third-order polynomial in $n_{jt}$. This follows the correction proposed in \textcite{backusWhyProductivityCorrelated2020}, where order statistics can be spuriously correlated with group size when the number of firms is small relative to the number of quantiles.

I then study heterogeneity in the pro-competitive effect by interacting predicted import penetration with lagged domestic concentration measures such as HHI, CR10, and CR4:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\rho_{jt}^{(k)} &= \beta_{O1}^{(k)} \widehat{\Delta IP_{jt}} + \beta_{O2}^{(k)} \widehat{\Delta IP_{jt}} \times \text{Concentration}_{jt-1} + \beta_I^{(k)} Z_{jt}^{\text{Input}}  + \gamma_2^{(k)} \text{LaborShare}_{jt_0} \\
&\quad + \gamma_1^{(k)} \text{LaborShare}_{jt_0} \times \text{Post2016}_t + X'_{jt-1} \gamma^{(k)} + \tau_t^{(k)} + \delta_j^{(k)} + g^{(k)}(n_{jt}) + \nu_{jt}^{(k)}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

This specification tests whether import competition exerts stronger disciplinary effects in sectors where domestic market power is initially more concentrated. The same logic is extended to additional mechanism tests using interactions with baseline sectoral characteristics, such as initial markup levels, size, or revenue growth.

\section{Results}

\subsection{First stage and identification diagnostics}

I begin by assessing the relevance of the output-side instrument and the empirical distinctness of the output and input channels. Table \ref{tab:firststage} reports the first-stage estimates from regressing sectoral Chinese import penetration on the output-competition instrument \(Z^{\text{Output}}_{jt}\), while controlling for the input-supply shifter \(Z^{\text{Input}}_{jt}\), sector fixed effects, and year fixed effects. I report both a parsimonious specification and the baseline specification that additionally includes lagged firm-level controls. Because the treatment varies at the sector-year level, the firm-year observation count overstates the amount of independent variation; the effective design contains 609 sector-year cells.

The first-stage results show that the instrument is strongly predictive of realized import-penetration changes. In both specifications, the coefficient on \(Z^{\text{Output}}_{jt}\) is positive and precisely estimated, with an estimate of 0.892 and a standard error of 0.179. The corresponding excluded-instrument first-stage \(F\)-statistic is about 24.8 in both columns, well above conventional weak-instrument thresholds. This indicates that the variation in Chinese export growth to other high-income destinations provides a strong source of identifying variation for import competition in Turkish manufacturing sectors.

By contrast, the coefficient on the input-supply control \(Z^{\text{Input}}_{jt}\) is small and statistically insignificant in the first stage. This pattern is useful for interpretation. It suggests that, conditional on sector and year fixed effects, the main predictive power for sectoral import penetration comes from the output-side shift-share instrument rather than from the upstream input channel. In other words, the first stage is not being driven by input-cost comovement.

A related concern is whether the two shifters are too closely aligned to be interpreted as distinct channels. The table therefore also reports the small correlation between \(Z^{\text{Output}}_{jt}\) and \(Z^{\text{Input}}_{jt}\). This positive but modest correlation indicates that the two measures are related, as one would expect given their common connection to China-driven trade shocks, but far from collinear. In the robustness, orthogonalizing the output shock with respect to the input-supply channel does not change any coefficients of interest. This supports the strategy of using \(Z^{\text{Output}}_{jt}\) as the excluded instrument while including \(Z^{\text{Input}}_{jt}\) as a control for the input-supply channel.

Finally, the near-identical first-stage estimates across the parsimonious and baseline specifications indicate that the predictive content of the instrument is not sensitive to the inclusion of lagged firm characteristics. This stability is reassuring for the subsequent IV analysis. At the same time, the combination of a moderate number of sector-year cells and concentrated Rotemberg weights implies that the estimates should be read as local to influential exposed sectors. Overall, the first-stage evidence supports the relevance of the output-side instrument and clarifies the scope of the identifying variation used in the baseline specifications.

At the same time, column (1) shows that import competition also affects observed survival in the panel. The positive and significant coefficient in the observed-exit regression indicates that sectors more exposed to Chinese import penetration experience more sample disappearance. This matters for identification because the observed sample of markup changes is conditional on survival. If import competition disproportionately removes firms with systematically different markup dynamics, conventional IV estimates on surviving firms may conflate within-firm pricing responses with composition effects arising from endogenous selection.

To address this issue, column (4) implements the selection-correction procedure based on a control function in the spirit of Backus (2020), using lagged EBIT margin as a proxy for the latent firm state governing observed survival and subsequent adjustment decisions. The corrected estimate remains negative and significant, implying that the decline in markup adjustments is not solely an artifact of sample selection. Hence, even after accounting for endogenous survival in the observed panel, the evidence supports a genuine within-firm disciplining effect of import competition on markups.

Comparing columns (3) and (4) further suggests that selection bias is economically relevant but not dominant. Once lagged firm controls are included, the coefficient on import penetration is $-0.462$; after adding the polynomial control-function correction, it becomes $-0.454$. The stability of the estimate indicates that conditioning on observed survival does not overturn the baseline conclusion, although it remains important to account for selection explicitly when panel disappearance responds to the same trade shock. Put differently, Chinese import competition appears to reduce markup growth among surviving firms, while also reshaping which firms remain observed in the sample.

Finally, the coefficient on the input-supply shock is statistically insignificant in all markup specifications. This suggests that, in this setting, the output-competition channel is the main force behind firm-level pricing adjustment, whereas upstream input-supply effects do not generate a robust independent effect on markup changes. Substantively, the results imply that Chinese import exposure primarily disciplines domestic firms through direct competitive pressure in output markets rather than through changes in access to imported inputs.

\subsection{From firm to sector}

Table \ref{tab:decomposition_results_alt} connects the firm-level evidence to sector-level markup dynamics by decomposing changes in inverse sector markups into four components: within-firm adjustment, reallocation across continuing firms, observed entry, and observed exit. The sectoral markup is constructed as the inverse of the market-share-weighted average of firm-level inverse markups, so that changes in sectoral market power reflect both individual pricing responses and changes in the composition of firms and market shares. This aggregation follows the harmonic-mean logic emphasized in the recent markup literature.

Panel A shows that, under the sector-level IV specification, the estimated effect of output import competition on the total change in sector-level markups is small and statistically insignificant. The same is true for each individual component. In particular, the coefficient on within-firm adjustment is close to zero, while the reallocation and observed-entry margins are negative and the observed-exit margin is positive, but all are imprecisely estimated. Taken together, these estimates suggest that, once firm-level responses are aggregated to the sector level, the pro-competitive effect of Chinese import penetration does not translate into a precisely estimated change in sectoral markups. By contrast, the input-supply shock is associated with a significant increase in inverse sector markups, which is equivalent to lower sectoral markup growth. This effect is driven almost entirely by the reallocation component: sectors more exposed to upstream input shocks experience a statistically significant shift in market shares toward firms with higher inverse markups, or lower markups. The within-firm, observed-entry, and observed-exit terms are all statistically insignificant.

\subsection{Distributional heterogeneity}

Average effects can conceal substantial heterogeneity in firms' responses to import competition. To examine how the trade shock affects different parts of the markup-adjustment distribution, I estimate grouped IV quantile regressions at the sector-year level. The results reveal a clear asymmetric pattern. Across the central part of the distribution, from the 10th to roughly the 70th percentile, the estimated effects of Chinese import competition on markup adjustments are close to zero. By contrast, the coefficients become increasingly negative from the upper quartile onward, reaching about $-2.0$ at the 90th percentile. Figure \ref{fig:ivq_combined} shows that the pro-competitive effect is therefore concentrated in the upper tail of the distribution rather than spread uniformly across firms.

This pattern suggests that import competition primarily constrains large positive markup adjustments among firms located at the top of the distribution. In other words, the main within-firm discipline effect does not operate through a broad compression of all firms' pricing behavior, but through a disproportionate reduction in the ability of upper-tail firms to raise markups. Firms in the center of the distribution, which are already making small or moderate markup adjustments, appear much less responsive to the trade shock. This finding is consistent with models in which competition bites most strongly for firms enjoying greater ex ante pricing power.

I next examine whether this upper-tail compression depends on domestic market structure. To do so, I interact import penetration with a measure of pre-period sectoral concentration, proxied by the domestic CR4 ratio. The estimates, reported in Figure \ref{fig:ivq_combined}, show that the negative upper-tail effects are stronger in more concentrated sectors. The interaction coefficients remain close to zero in the lower and central quantiles, but become increasingly negative at high quantiles when concentration is high. This indicates that the compression of the markup-adjustment distribution is amplified in markets where a small number of firms account for a large share of domestic sales.

Taken together, these results support a mechanism in which import competition disciplines the firms with the greatest scope to adjust markups upward, especially in concentrated domestic industries. The evidence is therefore more consistent with targeted compression at the top of the distribution than with a uniform decline in markups across all firms. This also helps reconcile the firm-level and sector-level results: even when average sectoral effects appear modest, import competition may still exert economically meaningful pro-competitive pressure by restricting the pricing behavior of high-markup firms.

\section{Robustness checks}

\subsection{Identification robustness}

Table \ref{tab:rob12_fe} reports robustness checks of the main IV estimate using alternative functional forms and progressively reduced control sets. In the baseline specification (column 1), import penetration has a negative and statistically significant effect on markup growth (coefficient: -0.462), and this result remains significant under wild-cluster bootstrap inference (p = 0.026). Adding a quadratic term for import penetration (column 2) does not provide evidence of meaningful nonlinearity, as the squared term is imprecisely estimated. Across columns 3 to 9, the coefficient on import penetration remains consistently negative and broadly similar in magnitude as controls are removed step-by-step, indicating that the main result is not driven by a specific control choice. Overall, the table supports the robustness of the core finding that greater import penetration is associated with lower markup adjustments.

Rotemberg weights are concentrated, which is consistent with the granular structure of Turkish manufacturing and the oligopolistic mechanism emphasized in the model. Excluding the most influential sectors sharply weakens the first stage, indicating that the identifying variation is economically concentrated rather than broadly diffuse (Table \ref{tab:rob_rotemberg_exclusions}). I therefore treat the baseline estimate as credible but local to influential exposed sectors, not as evidence that the same magnitude necessarily applies uniformly across all manufacturing sectors.


\subsection{Alternative markup measures}
I implement the \textcite{kirovMeasuringMarkupsRevenue2026} methodology (Appendix A.2) as a robustness check to the baseline measurement. Table \ref{tab:kirov_iv} reports these estimates. Because this approach requires estimating markup levels using lagged markup shifters and a Markov process for revenue productivity, the usable firm-year sample falls substantially relative to the baseline. When markup adjustments are then constructed from these estimated levels, the sample contracts further in a non-random way (about 25\% sample loss). The resulting estimates are therefore illustrative rather than decisive. Even so, the point estimates remain negative and of similar magnitude to the baseline, which suggests that the main findings are not purely an artifact of the baseline markup measure.

Re-estimating the baseline under tighter and looser trimming of extreme markup changes does not alter the sign or broad magnitude of the main coefficient (Table \ref{tab:rob_trimming}), which suggests that the results are not driven by outliers in the markup-adjustment distribution. The distributional results are similarly stable under alternative trimming choices: the negative effect remains concentrated in the upper tail.

\subsection{Selection and panel composition}
Table \ref{tab:rob_longlived} examines increasingly selective subsamples of longer-lived firms. The negative markup coefficient attenuates and becomes less precise when the sample is restricted to firms with longer observed panel histories. This is the expected direction: conditioning on long-lived firms removes part of the compositional margin through which import competition changes the observed sample. For that reason, the baseline specification based on the full unbalanced panel remains the preferred estimate of within-incumbent discipline in the observed data.

\subsection{Residualizing the input supply channel}
As an additional check, I residualize the output-competition instrument with respect to the input-supply shock and re-estimate the main specifications using only the orthogonalized component of the output shock. This addresses the concern that the main IV could be picking up variation shared with the input channel rather than purely output-market competitive pressure. Appendix Tables \ref{tab:rob_resid_fs}--\ref{tab:rob_resid_aux} report the first-stage comparison, the baseline markup specification, the control-set sensitivity exercise, and the auxiliary-outcome regressions.

The results are strikingly stable. In the baseline markup equation, the coefficient on import penetration is unchanged when the residualized instrument replaces the original output IV: the point estimate remains $-0.462$ with the same standard error and the same first-stage strength (Appendix Table \ref{tab:rob_resid_main}). The same stability appears in the auxiliary outcomes. Revenue growth and domestic-sales growth remain negative and statistically significant, while export growth remains negative but imprecisely estimated (Appendix Table \ref{tab:rob_resid_aux}). In other words, purging the output instrument of its linear overlap with $Z^{\text{Input}}$ does not alter the qualitative or quantitative conclusions.

This robustness check strengthens the interpretation of the main estimates as evidence for an output-competition channel. The negative markup response is not an artifact of correlation between the two shift-share shocks; rather, it survives when identification comes only from the component of output competition orthogonal to the input-supply shock. Together with the mechanism exercises, this supports the view that Chinese import exposure primarily disciplined firms through product-market competition rather than through contamination from the input-cost channel.

\subsection{Distributional design robustness}
I report three additional distributional checks in Appendix Figure \ref{fig:rob41_decquin}--\ref{fig:rob43_thin}. First, comparing grouped IVQ estimates built on deciles versus quintiles yields very similar profiles: coefficients are close to zero through the middle of the distribution and turn more negative in the upper tail, indicating that the main pattern is not driven by bin choice (Figure \ref{fig:rob41_decquin}). Second, I re-rank observations by lagged markup level instead of markup change. The resulting coefficients remain weak in most bins and become most negative near the top ranks, confirming that upper-tail discipline is not specific to the baseline ranking metric (Figure \ref{fig:rob42_rank}). Third, I exclude thin sector-year cells and compare cutoffs for minimum cell size. The estimated quantile profile is nearly unchanged across these restrictions, with the strongest negative effects still concentrated at high quantiles (Figure \ref{fig:rob43_thin}).


\section{Mechanisms}

\subsection{Indirect evidence of residual-demand effects}
To indirectly test the residual-demand channel, I augment the baseline IV specification by interacting Chinese import penetration with lagged firm market-power proxies (lagged markup level). In reduced form, the estimated equation is
\[
\Delta \ln \mu_{ijt} = \beta_1 \Delta IP_{jt} + \beta_2 (\Delta IP_{jt} \times \mu_{ijt-1}) + \Gamma X_{ijt-1} + \alpha_i + \delta_{jt} + \varepsilon_{ijt},
\]

The estimates in Table \ref{tab:mech_lagged_interactions} show a negative baseline effect of import penetration on markup growth. The interaction with lagged markup is positive and economically significant, implying that the disciplining effect becomes less negative at higher initial markup levels. This is not a clean confirmation of a simple ``high-markup firms are hit most'' narrative. Even so, the implied marginal effects remain negative at the 25th, 50th, and 75th percentiles of lagged markup. I therefore treat this interaction exercise as supportive but partial evidence on residual-demand effects, and I place more weight on the grouped-IV quantile results for the claim that discipline is concentrated in the upper tail of markup adjustments.

\section{Conclusion}

This thesis studies how Chinese import competition reshapes market power in Turkish manufacturing by separating output-competition from input-supply effects and by distinguishing within-firm markup adjustment from compositional change. Using ORBIS/AMADEUS firm data, a shift-share instrument based on Chinese exports to other high-income markets, an input-supply shifter built from frozen input-output linkages, and complementary evidence from selection correction, grouped IV quantiles, and sectoral decomposition, I find a clear pro-competitive effect at the firm level. Higher Chinese import penetration reduces markup growth among surviving firms, with stronger effects in the upper tail of the markup-adjustment distribution and in more concentrated sectors. The input-supply channel does not robustly raise within-firm markups, but it does contribute to reallocation toward lower-markup firms in sectors more exposed to Chinese inputs.

The aggregate interpretation is narrower than the original framing of ``creative destruction.'' Import competition raises observed exit probabilities in the panel, yet the selection correction shows that the within-incumbent disciplining effect is not driven by survival bias. At the sector level, total markup effects are weak because within-firm discipline is partly offset by reallocation across continuing firms, while observed entry and exit contribute little in the decomposition. Since ORBIS disappearance is not validated firm death, the safest conclusion is that Chinese import competition disciplines markup growth among observed incumbents and reshuffles market shares among survivors; it is not, in this thesis alone, a definitive test of Schumpeterian creative destruction.

Several limitations remain. Markups are inferred from accounting data and therefore depend on production-function assumptions and sample coverage. The shift-share design is informative but local to influential exposed sectors. The reduced-form evidence is also stronger on incumbent pricing than on welfare or broad market-power control. For that reason, the results should be read as suggestive for competition policy rather than as showing that trade liberalization and antitrust are straightforward substitutes. Future research could combine customs, product-price, employment, and business-demography data to validate exit, connect markup compression to consumer prices and wages, and extend the analysis to longer-run productivity and entry dynamics.

\subsection{Markup Estimation}

\subsubsection{Setup}

I estimate firm markups using the production approach \parencite{deridderHitchhikersGuideMarkup2026,deloeckerMarkupsFirmLevelExport2012}. For firm $i$ in year $t$, define:
\begin{align*}
r_{it} &= \log R_{it} \quad \text{(log revenue)} \\
x_{it} &= \log X_{it} \quad \text{(log COGS)} \\
k_{it} &= \log K_{it} \quad \text{(log capital)}
\end{align*}

Sample restrictions: $R_{it} > 0$, $X_{it} > 0$, $K_{it} > 0$.

For a flexible (variable) input, the production approach gives:
\[
\mu_{it} = \alpha_{it}^{\text{COGS}} \cdot \frac{R_{it}}{X_{it}}
\]

In logs:
\[
\log \hat{\mu}_{it} = \log \hat{\alpha}_{it}^{\text{COGS}} + \log R_{it} - \log X_{it}
\]

\subsubsection{Estimating the Output Elasticity of COGS}

\paragraph{Cobb--Douglas (constant elasticity)}

Estimate within ISIC4:
\[
r_{it} = \beta_x x_{it} + \beta_k k_{it} + \lambda_t + \varepsilon_{it}
\]

Then $\hat{\alpha}_{it}^{\text{COGS}} = \hat{\beta}_x$.

\paragraph{Two-input Translog (elasticity varies with input levels)}

Estimate:
\[
r_{it} = \beta_x x_{it} + \beta_k k_{it} + \beta_{xx} x_{it}^2 + \beta_{kk} k_{it}^2 + \beta_{xk} x_{it} k_{it} + \lambda_t + \varepsilon_{it}
\]

Then the firm-year COGS elasticity is:
\[
\hat{\alpha}_{it}^{\text{COGS}} = \frac{\partial r_{it}}{\partial x_{it}} = \hat{\beta}_x + 2\hat{\beta}_{xx} x_{it} + \hat{\beta}_{xk} k_{it}
\]

\subsection{Implementing Kirov et al. (2026) methodology as robustness check}

KMT is designed to treat omitted-price bias and allow markup heterogeneity across firms and over time. KMT methodology consists of two steps: first, recover a revenue elasticity, which is contaminated by the markup, from the flexible-input share equation; second, use a Markov process for revenue productivity to back out the true physical output elasticity and thus the markup level. The KMT approach is more robust to misspecification of the production function and allows for more flexible markup dynamics, but it also relies on stronger assumptions about the evolution of productivity and the structure of demand.

\textbf{Revenue production function}: In the implemented robustness, I use a Cobb-Douglas baseline:
\[r_{it} = \beta_x x_{it} + \beta_k k_{it} + \nu_{it} + \varepsilon_{it}\]
where $\nu_{it}$ is the revenue productivity shock that follows a first-order Markov process: $\nu_{it} = \rho \nu_{it-1} + \eta_{it}$, and $\varepsilon_{it}$ is an i.i.d. shock. The key difference from the standard production function estimation is that the dependent variable is revenue rather than physical output, which means that the estimated $\beta_x$ captures both the physical output elasticity with respect to the flexible input and the markup. 

\textbf{Revenue productivity Markov process}: To identify the physical output elasticity, KMT exploits the first-order Markov structure of revenue productivity as follows:
\[\nu_{it} = \rho g(\nu_{it-1}) + \eta_{it}\]
where $g(\cdot)$ is a non-parametric function that captures the potentially non-linear evolution of revenue productivity. I approximate $g(\cdot)$ using a third-order polynomial in lagged revenue productivity.

\textbf{Markup control function}: Let markups be a function of inputs, time and firm fixed effects, and a vector of both firm- and sector-level time-varying parameters $\mathbb{D}_{i(j)t-1}$ that shift markups but are not included in the production function estimation. These parameters include lagged share of sales, lagged exporter status, and lagged HHI at the ISIC4 level, which are all potential confounders of the markup estimation. 

\textbf{Share equation}: I then regress the log flexible-input cost share in revenue ($s^x_{it} \equiv cx_{it} - r_{it}$) on log inputs and markup shifters:
\[s^x_{it} = \ln \beta_x - \mu_{it} + b - \varepsilon_{it}\]
where $\ln(\beta_x)$ is the technological component, which captures the effect of the flexible input on revenue. $\mu_{it}$ is the markup component, which captures the effect of markups on the cost share. Higher markups lower the cost share, so it enters with a negative sign. $b-\varepsilon_{it}$ is the error term, which states that even after firms choose inputs, realized cost shares may differ from the predicted cost shares due to measurement error or other unobserved factors.

In my analysis, the share equation is estimated as follows:
\[\ln(\text{COGS}_{it}) - \ln(R_{it}) = \ln{\beta_{COGS}} - \big(\phi_0 + \phi_x x_{it} + \phi_k k_{it} + \phi_1 \text{share\_sales}_{it-1} + \phi_2 \text{exporter\_status}_{it-1} + \phi_3 \text{HHI}_{jt-1} + \tau_t + \delta_j + \lambda_i \big) + b - \varepsilon_{it}\]

\textbf{Final markup estimator}: After recovering $\hat{\theta}^{\text{COGS}} = \hat{\beta}_{\text{COGS}}$, I compute: 
\[\hat{\mu}_{it} = \frac{\hat{\theta}_{\text{COGS}}}{\text{COGS}_it / R_{it}}\]
then define the markup adjustment as $\Delta \hat{\mu}_{it} = \hat{\mu}_{it} - \hat{\mu}_{it-1}$, which is the dependent variable in the main regression analysis.
