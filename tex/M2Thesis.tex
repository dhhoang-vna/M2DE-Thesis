\textbf{Abstract}

\smallskip

This paper empirically studies how Chinese import competition affects the dynamics of market power in Turkish manufacturing, motivated by a parsimonious framework of oligopolistic competition, variable markups, and input-output networks. Using firm-level financial data and a classic shift-share design, I disentangle the China output-competition and input-supply channels, and address firms' endogenous exit to identify how incumbents respond through within-firm markup adjustment and across-incumbent reallocation of market shares. The disciplining effect is strongest in the most concentrated sectors. The findings suggest that trade liberalization and domestic competition policy are partial substitutes for controlling market power, with important implications for emerging markets facing rising import competition and declining business dynamism.

\smallskip
\textit{Keywords}: Oligopoly, variable markups, shift-share instrument, import competition, firm dynamics, emerging markets

\section{Introduction}

Oligopoly is widespread in many industries, yet empirical evidence on how foreign competition affects market power in oligopolistic environments is limited, particularly in emerging markets where barriers to entry and exit can be more pronounced. Leveraging the Turkish manufacturing sectors under import competition from China, this paper reexamines the effects of the "China shocks" and highlights the distinct pro-competitive effects from foreign competition through strategic interactions between incumbents, specifically through within-firm adjustment of markups to strategically defend market position, and through across-incumbent reallocation of market shares. Each is promoted by a different channel of import exposure: the former is driven by the output-competition channel, while the latter is driven by the input-supply channel. Importantly, the selection effect from endogenous exit is negligible, which emphasizes that the pro-competitive effect of import competition is mostly driven by the adjustment of observed incumbent firms.

Turkey is a useful setting for this study. Since the upgrading of bilateral political relations in 2010, Turkish manufacturing has faced rising Chinese import penetration within an already concentrated sector and a structurally asymmetric trade relationship marked by a persistent bilateral deficit and strong dependence on imported intermediates. For Turkish firms, Chinese imports affect both product-market competition and input costs, making the effect on markups theoretically ambiguous: stronger final-good competition may compress market power, while cheaper intermediate inputs may lower marginal costs and support higher markups for input-using firms.\footnote{\textcite{aghionOpposingFirmLevelResponses2024} emphasizes these opposing firm-level responses to Chinese import exposure.} Markups are therefore the key outcome through which trade shocks map into consumer prices, allocative efficiency, rent distribution, and industrial dynamics.

Trade models with variable markups imply a pro-competitive effect in perfect competition and monopolistic competition.\footnote{Tougher import competition increases the elasticity of residual demand and compresses markups \parencite{melitzMarketSizeTrade2008, edmondCompetitionMarkupsGains2015}} In contrast, once survival is endogenous, observed average markups can be stable or rise if low-markup firms disproportionately exit and the surviving set is tilted toward high-markup incumbents \parencite{arkolakisElusiveProCompetitiveEffects2019}. Empirically, these forces are easy to confound. A regression of markup changes on import penetration can mix between within-incumbent pricing responses to competitive pressure, and compositional changes in the observed sample caused by endogenous exit. In fact, attrition can remain correlated with unobservables that also drive firms' adjustment of markups. These ambiguities map directly into an identification problem. Furthermore, import shocks can have distributional effects within the firm population. Even if the average markup response is negative, that average can conceal firm heterogeneity.

To clarify these ambiguities, I construct a parsimonious model of oligopolistic competition with variable markups and input-output linkages, which delivers testable predictions on how Chinese import penetration affects firm-level markups, market shares, and sectoral markups through both output competition and input supply channels. Then, I test these theoretical predictions using the Turkish manufacturing firm-level dataset from ORBIS/AMADEUS database, combined with different sector-level datasets from CEPII (France), Turkish Statistical Institute (TURKSTAT), World Input-Output Database (WIOD), and United Nations Industrial Development Organization (UNIDO). The ORBIS/AMADEUS database is compiled by the Bureau van Dijk Electronic Publishing (BvD), a private-sector organization that seeks to compile accounting information on companies from all over the world. 

Leveraging these data, I implement a unified pipeline of four components. First, I identify the causal effect of Chinese penetration of final goods on firm-level markup adjustments using a shift-share IV design. From this instrument, I construct a control variable to isolate the input-supply channel using input-output linkage intensity between sectors. Second, I address endogenous firm exit using a flexible selection-correction approach motivated by dynamic firm models. Third, I use grouped IV quantile regression to study the distributional heterogeneity of markup responses across firms, particularly whether trade induced discipline is concentrated in particular parts of the conditional distribution of markup adjustments. Finally, to connect micro responses to sectoral outcomes, I decompose changes in sectoral markups into within-firm, reallocation, and selection components using a harmonic-mean aggregation framework, and test the effects of output- and input-shock channels to each component.

Overall, results reveal three key findings. First, Chinese import penetration of final goods, through reducing residual demand, decreases Turkish firms' markup growth on average, and this effect concentrates among firms with high growth of markups, market shares, and initial markup levels. Second, the input-supply channel does not have any significant effect on firm-level markup adjustments, but it reallocates market shares toward firms with lower markups, particularly for firms with stronger input linkages to China. This cost channel partially offsets the disciplining effect of output competition. Third, the selection effect is negligible, emphasizing that the pro-competitive effect of import competition is mostly driven by the adjustment of incumbent firms. All results are aligned with the theoretical framework and robust to a number of robustness checks. 

Together, the import shocks, through these disentangled mechanisms, heighten strategic rivalry across surviving firms in Turkey manufacturing. More broadly, the results indicate that trade liberalization and domestic antitrust policy can be partially substitutable to control the rise of market power in a dynamic oligopoly environment. The findings have important implications for emerging markets facing rising import competition and declining business dynamism, as they suggest that trade shocks can have complex effects on market power that depend on the interplay between competitive pressure, cost changes, and firm dynamics.

The paper contributes to some literature strands. First, quantitative trade literature with oligopolistic competition and variable markups emphasize that trade costs and foreign competition can shift markups through market-share movements and variable pass-through \parencite{deblasUnderstandingMarkupsOpen2015,atkesonPricingtoMarketTradeCosts2008,gaubertGranularComparativeAdvantage2021}. I extend this literature by extending the theoretical model with intermediate input and production network to guide the empirical strategy.

The second literature is China shock and its impact. Beginning with the seminal \autocite{autorChinaSyndromeLocal2013}, this empirical literature leverages a shift-share design to estimate the causal effects of rising Chinese import competition.\footnote{Much of this work focuses on labor markets, employment, and local outcomes \parencite{autorTradeAdjustmentWorkerLevel2014, acemogluImportCompetitionGreat2016,pierceSurprisinglySwiftDecline2016}, some focus on innovation and productivity outcomes \parencite{aghionOpposingFirmLevelResponses2024, bloomTradeInducedTechnical2016}.}. Some studies focus the impact of trade and trade policy on market power \parencite{deloeckerPricesMarkupsTrade2016,impullittiGlobalizationMarketPower2023}, consumer prices \parencite{jaravelWhatArePrice}, and concentration \parencite{amitiUSMarketConcentration2025}. However, these studies do not causally identify the effects of import competition on the dynamics of market power at different levels, and its underlying mechanisms. This paper contributes to this literature by providing firm-level evidence on this gap.

The empirical question in this paper is inherently about firm and industry dynamics \parencite{melitzImpactTradeIntraIndustry2003,ericsonMarkovPerfectIndustryDynamics1995,hopenhaynEntryExitFirm1992}: competition affects both firms' behavior and survival. Turkey's documented decline in business dynamism make this particularly relevant \parencite{akcigitFactsBusinessDynamism2020}. By explicitly correcting for endogenous exit, the paper contributes a flexible solution to a broader class of problems in which observed firm outcomes are selected by survival, and where separating within-firm effects from compositional change is necessary for interpretation. 

The paper also contributes to the literature on the distributional effects of trade shocks\footnote{\textcite{chetverikovIVQuantileRegression2016} proposes a grouped-IV quantile methodology and its application in the case of China shocks. \textcite{galleSlicingPieQuantifying2023} quantifies the group-level welfare effect of trade.} and markup dynamics\footnote{\textcite{bursteinBottomUpMarkupFluctuations2025} shows the comovement of firm, sector, and aggregate markups with output in response to firm-level shocks. \textcite{baqaeeProductivityMisallocationGeneral2020} quantitatively shows that within-firm changes in markups are important in the accounting of movements in aggregate markups.} by showing that foreign competition on final goods disproportionately affects firms with initial higher markups and sectors with higher concentration, while inputs-supply shocks disproportionately reallocate market shares out of largest firms and toward firms with stronger input linkages to China. The two channels together heighten strategic rivalry across surviving firms in Turkey manufacturing, and the results suggest that trade liberalization and domestic antitrust policy can be partially substitutable to control the rise of market power in a dynamic oligopoly environment.

The rest of the paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 delivers a theoretical framework. Section 3 presents data and measurement, including the construction of output- and input-exposure variables, markup estimation, and market-share-weighted harmonic means of firm markups. Section 4 develops the empirical strategy. Section 5 presents the main results, including shift-share IV baseline, selection correction, grouped-IV quantile estimates and sectoral decomposition. Section 6 provides the robustness checks and sensitivity analysis. Section 7 highlights some relevant mechanisms to better connect empirical results with theoretical predictions. Section 8 concludes.

\section{Theoretical framework}

\subsection{Model}
This section develops a tractable oligopoly model with variable markups, following \textcite{atkesonPricingtoMarketTradeCosts2008}, which links Chinese import shocks to firm-level markups, market shares, and sectoral market power in Turkish manufacturing. Here I drop time $t$ for simplicity. 

\textbf{Environment}: Time is discrete, indexed by $t=0,1,2,...$. The economy contains a continuum of manufacturing sectors indexed by $j \in [0,1]$. In each sector, there is a finite set of domestic firms $i = 1, ..., N_j$ that produce differentiated varieties and compete in quantities à la Cournot. The number of active domestic firms is endogenous because firms may exit after observing current profitability and the continuation value of staying active. 

Domestic firms sell differentiated varieties in the domestic market and compete strategically. Foreign producers affect the domestic equilibrium through two distinct channels. First, through the output-competition channel, imported final goods compete directly with domestic production in the domestic product market, which affects the residual demand faced by domestic firms and therefore their optimal markups. Second, through the input-supply channel, imported intermediate inputs affect domestic firms' marginal costs through direct and indirect sourcing linkages, which affects production costs and therefore equilibrium prices, quantities, shares, and exit decisions. Let $\tau^O_j, \tau^I_j$ denote the output-competition and input-supply states in sector $j$, respectively. In the dynamic setting, each active firm $i$ is characterized by the state vector $x_{ijt}$ that includes its productivity, demand shifters, lagged market share, the import shocks $\tau^O_{jt}, \tau^I_{jt}$, and other relevant state variables.

\textbf{Final demand}: A representative household supplies inelastic labor and consumes a final aggregate $C$ constructed from sectoral composites $Q_j$: 
\[C = \Bigg(\int_{0}^{1} \alpha_j^{\frac{1}{\eta}} Q_{jt}^{\frac{\eta-1}{\eta}}dj \Bigg)^{\frac{\eta}{\eta-1}}, \quad \eta > 1, \int_{0}^{1} \alpha_j dj = 1\]
where $\alpha_j$ is the preference weight for sector $j$ and $\eta$ is the elasticity of substitution between sectors. Let $P_{j}$ denote the sectoral price index and $P$ the consumer CES price index. Standard aggregation implies $P = \bigg(\int_{0}^{1} \alpha_j P_{jt}^{1-\eta} dj \bigg)^{\frac{1}{1-\eta}}$. Given total nominal expenditure $Y_t$, sectoral expenditure (domestic absorption) in sector $j$ is given by $D_{j} = \alpha_j \bigg(\frac{P_j}{P}\bigg)^{1-\eta} Y_t$. 

\textbf{Demand side for domestic firms}: Within each sector, domestic absorption $D_{jt}$ is split between domestic and foreign suppliers (China plus the rest of the world). Let $\Lambda_j^D \in (0,1]$ denote the CES weight on the domestic composite in sector $j$, so that the foreign weight is $1-\Lambda_j^D$. Use a nested CES representation:
$$Q_{jt} = \bigg[ (\Lambda_j^D)^{\frac{1}{\nu_j}} (Q_{jt}^D)^{\frac{\nu_j-1}{\nu_j}} + (1-\Lambda_j^D)^{\frac{1}{\nu_j}} (Q_{jt}^F)^{\frac{\nu_j-1}{\nu_j}} \bigg]^{\frac{\nu_j}{\nu_j-1}}$$

where $Q_{jt}^D$ and $Q_{jt}^F$ are the domestic and foreign composites, respectively, and $\nu_j > 1$ is the elasticity of substitution between domestic and foreign varieties. For a given change in domestic prices, a higher foreign weight means stronger substitution towards foreign varieties, which increases the elasticity of residual demand faced by domestic firms. Assume $\rho_j > \nu_j > \eta$, so that the elasticity of substitution between domestic varieties is higher than the elasticity between domestic and foreign varieties, which is in turn higher than the elasticity between sectors. This assumption implies that the pro-competitive effect of import competition is stronger for firms with higher market shares and in more concentrated sectors.

Within the domestic segment of sector $j$, consumers have CES preferences over the differentiated varieties supplied by domestic firms $i = 1, ..., N_j$:
\[Q_{j}^D = \Bigg(\sum_{i=1}^{N_j} \xi_i^{\frac{1}{\rho_j}} q_{ij}^{\frac{\rho_j-1}{\rho_j}} \Bigg)^{\frac{\rho_j}{\rho_j-1}}, \quad \rho_j > 1\]

where $\rho_j$ is the elasticity of substitution between domestic varieties, $\xi_i$ is the variety-specific share parameter, and $q_{ij}$ is the quantity of variety $i$ in sector $j$. The associated domestic price index is $P_j^D = \bigg(\sum_{i=1}^{N_j} \xi_i p_{ij}^{1-\rho_j} \bigg)^{\frac{1}{1-\rho_j}}$. Conditional on $Q_j^D$, the demand facing firm $i$ is $q_{ij} = \xi_i \bigg(\frac{p_{ij}}{P_j^D}\bigg)^{-\rho_j} Q_j^D$. Then, we can define the within-domestic sales share of firm $i$ as $\tilde{s}_{ij} = \frac{p_{ij} q_{ij}}{P_{j}^D Q_{j}^D}$.

\textbf{Production and costs}: Each domestic firm produces using labor and an intermediate input bundle according to a Cobb-Douglas technology: $Y_{ij} = \varphi_{ij} L_{ij}^{\beta_j} M_{ij}^{1-\beta_j}$, with $0 < \beta_j < 1$, where $\varphi_{ij}$ is firm productivity, $L_{ij}$ is labor input, and $M_{ij}$ is the intermediate input bundle, and $\beta_j$ is the labor share in production. Let $w$ denote the wage and $P^M_j$ the price index of the intermediate-input bundle. Under cost minimization with competitive factor markets, the exact nominal marginal cost of firm $i$ in sector $j$ is
\[
\text{mc}_{ij}
=
\frac{1}{\varphi_{ij}}
\left( \frac{w}{\beta_j} \right)^{\beta_j}
\left( \frac{P^M_j}{1-\beta_j} \right)^{1-\beta_j},
\]
so, up to the sector-specific constant $\beta_j^{-\beta_j}(1-\beta_j)^{-(1-\beta_j)}$, the simpler proportionality used below is $\text{mc}_{ij} \propto \varphi_{ij}^{-1} w^{\beta_j} (P^M_j)^{1-\beta_j}$.

Taking logs and differentiating yields the change in log marginal cost:
\[\Delta \ln \text{mc}_{ij} = -\Delta \ln \varphi_{ij} + \beta_j \Delta \ln w + (1-\beta_j) \Delta \ln P^M_j\]
The intermediate bundle $M_{ij}$ is a composite of sectoral input bundles indexed by $k$, and each sectoral input price is itself a composite of domestic and Chinese sourcing shares. From CES aggregation and log-linearization, the log change in the intermediate-input price in sector $j$ can be written as $d \ln P^M_{j} \approx \sum_k l_{jk} \omega_k^{CHN} d \ln P^{CHN}_{k}$, where $l_{jk}$ is the $(j,k)$-element of the Leontief inverse of the input-output matrix, and $\omega_k^{CHN}$ is the Chinese sourcing weight. Thus, changes in Chinese input prices can feed into domestic marginal costs through network propagation and sourcing intensity. Substituting this approximation into the marginal cost equation gives: 
\begin{equation}
d \ln \text{mc}_{ij} \approx -d \ln \varphi_{ij} + \beta_j d \ln w + (1-\beta_j) \sum_k l_{jk} \omega_k^{CHN} d \ln P^{CHN}_{k}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Oligopoly and markups}: In each sector $j$, domestic firms compete à la Cournot, taking their marginal costs and the residual demand curve as given. Firm $i$'s profit is $\pi_{ij} = (p_{ij} - \text{mc}_{ij}) q_{ij} - f_{ij}$, where $f_{ij}$ is a period fixed cost. Because demand is nested CES, a firm's perceived own-price elasticity is endogenous and depends on within-domestic, domestic-foreign, and between-sector substitution patterns. Let $s_{ij}$ denote the share of firm $i$ in total sectoral sales, so that $s_{ij} = \Lambda^D_{j} \tilde{s}_{ij}$. Under Cournot competition, the perceived elasticity faced by firm $i$ is 
\[ \sigma^C_{ij} = \Bigg(\frac{1-\tilde{s}_{ij}}{\rho_j} + \frac{\tilde{s}_{ij} - s_{ij}}{\nu_j} + \frac{{s}_{ij}}{\eta} \Bigg)^{-1} \]
where $\nu_j$ is the elasticity between domestic and foreign composites within the sector. The firm's optimal markup is therefore $\mu_{ij} \equiv \frac{p_{ij}}{\text{mc}_{ij}} = \frac{\sigma^C_{ij}}{\sigma^C_{ij} - 1}$. This formulation implies that perceived demand elasticity is decreasing in firm size, while markups are increasing in market share. The Cournot markup condition is therefore easiest to write in inverse-markup form:
\begin{equation}
\frac{1}{\mu_{ij}} = \Bigg( 1- \frac{1}{\rho_j}\Bigg) - \Bigg[\frac{1}{\Lambda^D_{j}} \Bigg(\frac{1}{\nu_j} - \frac{1}{\rho_j}\Bigg) + \Bigg(\frac{1}{\eta} - \frac{1}{\nu_j} \Bigg) \Bigg] {s}_{ij}
\end{equation}
Equivalently, the markup itself is
\begin{equation}
\mu_{ij} = \frac{1}{\Big( 1- \frac{1}{\rho_j}\Big) - \Big[\frac{1}{\Lambda^D_{j}} \Big(\frac{1}{\nu_j} - \frac{1}{\rho_j}\Big) + \Big(\frac{1}{\eta} - \frac{1}{\nu_j} \Big) \Big] {s}_{ij}}
\end{equation}
Define the corresponding Herfindahl index as $\text{HHI}_j = \sum_i^{N_j} s^2_{ij}$ and define the sectoral average markup as the harmonic mean of firm markups weighted by market shares, $\mu_j = \bigg(\sum_i^{N_j} \frac{s_{ij}}{\mu_{ij}}\bigg)^{-1}$. Then the sectoral inverse markup can be expressed as a function of the HHI and the parameters of the model:
\begin{equation}
\frac{1}{\mu_j} = \Bigg( 1- \frac{1}{\rho_j}\Bigg) - \Bigg[\frac{1}{\Lambda^D_{j}} \Bigg( \frac{1}{\nu_j} - \frac{1}{\rho_j}\Bigg) + \Bigg(\frac{1}{\eta} - \frac{1}{\nu_j} \Bigg) \Bigg] \text{HHI}_j
\end{equation}
so that
\begin{equation}
\mu_j = \frac{1}{\Big( 1- \frac{1}{\rho_j}\Big) - \Big[\frac{1}{\Lambda^D_{j}} \Big( \frac{1}{\nu_j} - \frac{1}{\rho_j}\Big) + \Big(\frac{1}{\eta} - \frac{1}{\nu_j} \Big) \Big] \text{HHI}_j}
\end{equation}
Sectoral markups are therefore increasing in concentration. Moreover, the slope of the concentration-markup relationship depends on the domestic expenditure share $\Lambda^D_{j}$, which means stronger foreign competition alters the perceived elasticity relevant for high-share firms and therefore changes the markup responses to concentration.

\textbf{Dynamic extension with endogenous exit}: A firm that remains active in period $t$ receives current profits and retains the option value of future operation. Let $V(x_{ijt})$ denote the value of continuing. The firm's Bellman equation is: $$V(x_{ijt}) = \max \{ 0, \pi(x_{ijt}) + \beta \mathbb{E} [V(x_{ij,t+1}) \mid x_{ijt}] \}$$ where $\beta \in (0,1)$ is the discount factor. Exit yields normalized continuation value zero. The optimal exit rule is: $Exit_{ijt}=1 \Leftrightarrow \pi(x_{ijt}) + \beta \mathbb{E} [V(x_{ij,t+1}) \mid x_{ijt}] < 0 $. Equivalently, there exists a threshold function $\theta^*(x_{ijt})$ such that a firms exits if its latent profitability index lies below the cutoff: $\theta(x_{ijt}) < \theta^*(x_{ijt})$. 

This shows that trade shocks affect both current profitability and the continuation value, so any observed firm-level outcome among survivors is selected on unobserved state variables. Hence, the effects of trade shocks on survival is theoretically ambiguous. On the one hand, tougher import competition can reduce current profits for weak firms, tending to raise exit. On the other hand, firms that benefit from input-cost reductions can remain sufficiently profitable to stay active. Consequently, the model allows strong incumbent discipline to coexist with modest aggregate selection, and motivates the empirical strategy to separate these effects.

\textbf{Equilibrium}: Given sectoral demand shifters, input prices, and technology, a static sectoral equilibrium is a collection of prices, quantities, and markups, such that the households allocate expenditure across sectors and domestic varieties, firms choose outputs to maximize profit under Cournot competition, and goods markets clear. The equilibrium is characterized by the system of equations described above, which can be solved numerically given parameter values and functional forms. The model delivers predictions on how changes in Chinese import penetration, through both output competition and input supply channels, affect firm-level markups, market shares, and sectoral markups.

\subsection{Testable predictions}

The model yields the following testable implications.
\paragraph{Prediction 1.} Stronger Chinese import penetration in final goods lowers firm-level markups on average.  
A rise in foreign competition reduces the domestic expenditure share $\Lambda_j^D$ and raises the residual demand elasticity faced by domestic incumbents. From equation (2), this lowers the optimal markup of the representative domestic firm. Thus, holding technology and input costs fixed: $\frac{\partial \mu_{ij}}{\partial \tau_j^O} < 0$

\paragraph{Prediction 2.} The disciplining effect of output competition is stronger for large firms and in concentrated sectors.  
Because the perceived elasticity under Cournot competition depends on firm market share and sector concentration, the same increase in foreign competitive pressure compresses markups more strongly for firms with high initial market shares and in sectors with higher $HHI_j$. In this model, large firms face a larger change in residual demand elasticity when foreign competition intensifies. Hence,
\[
\frac{\partial^2 \mu_{ij}}{\partial \tau_j^O \partial s_{ij}} < 0
\qquad \text{and} \qquad
\frac{\partial^2 \mu_{j}}{\partial \tau_j^O \partial HHI_j} < 0,
\]
under the maintained parameter restrictions $\rho_j > \nu_j > \eta$.

\paragraph{Prediction 3.} Input-supply shocks operate primarily through marginal costs and market shares. A favorable Chinese input shock lowers the cost of intermediate inputs in proportion to sectoral input-output exposure. From equation (1): $\frac{\partial \ln mc_{ij}}{\partial \tau_j^I} < 0$ for firms in sectors with stronger exposure to Chinese intermediate inputs, where $\tau_j^I$ denotes the input-supply shock. Lower marginal costs improve firms' competitive position and tend to expand their equilibrium market shares, so that $\frac{\partial s_{ij}}{\partial \tau_j^I} > 0$ for sufficiently exposed firms. By contrast, the effect on within-firm markups is in general ambiguous: $\frac{\partial \mu_{ij}}{\partial \tau_j^I} \gtrless 0$. The reason is that lower marginal cost may raise markups mechanically, but it also induces equilibrium price adjustment and stronger competitive responses. Accordingly, the input-supply channel is expected to matter primarily for market-share reallocation rather than for average within-firm markup compression.

\paragraph{Prediction 4.} Sectoral markup changes need not mirror average firm-level markup changes. Even if output competition lowers many firms' markups, the aggregate sector response may be attenuated or amplified depending on whether market shares shift toward low-markup or high-markup firms. Thus, the effect of trade shocks on $\mu_j$ is in general a combination of within-firm discipline and between-firm reallocation.

\paragraph{Prediction 5.} Observed firm-level markup changes may then combine a within-incumbent response with a compositional effect induced by endogenous exit. This motivates the selection-correction strategy developed in Section 4.2.

\section{Data and Measurement}
In this section, I describe the data sources, define the samples and key variables I use in the analysis, and present summary statistics.

\subsection{Data sources}
\textbf{Firm-level data.}  The ORBIS/AMADEUS dataset contains financial accounting information from detailed harmonized balance sheets, income statements, and profit and loss accounts of firms. I use the Turkish manufacturing firms in the database from 2009 to 2023. The data is used to construct firm-level outcomes such as markups, exit probability, and controls such as firm size, firm age, liquidity ratio, leverage ratio, exporter status, and sectoral concentration measures. A concordance between the database's NACE Rev.2 sector codes and the 4-digit ISIC is used to map firm-level data to the sectoral level.

The dataset comprises an unbalanced panel of 15,957 firms with a total of 78,921 firm-year observations. The sample is restricted to manufacturing firms to focus on the sectors most exposed to Chinese import competition. I also apply standard data cleaning steps, such as dropping firms with missing or negative revenue, cost of goods sold, and winsorize variables at the 1st and 99th percentiles to mitigate the influence of outliers.

\textbf{Trade data.} My main variable is the import penetration rate from China over time and across 4-digit ISIC sectors, which is at time t defined as: 
\begin{equation}
    \begin{aligned}
    \text{China IP}_{jt} = \frac{\text{Import}_{jt}^{\text{CHN} \to \text{TR}}}{\text{Output}_{jt} + \text{Import}_{jt}^{\text{World} \to \text{TR}} - \text{Export}_{jt}^{\text{TR} \to \text{World}}} 
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where the denominator corresponds to domestic absorption. I construct the nominator of the variable using the CEPII BACI HS1 dataset, which provides bilateral trade flows at the HS 6-digit level, then map it to the 4-digit ISIC sectors using the HS-ISIC concordance (Liao et al., 2021). I also use the UNIDO ISDB4/INDSTAT4 database for the 4-digit sector denominators. The change of import penetration rate is the first difference of the above variable. These datasets are used also to construct the shift-share IV for output competition.

For the input supply channel, I construct the variable from the output shift-share IV weighted by the Turkey's input-output linkage intensity between 2-digit sectors. The linkage data is from WIOD. 

\textbf{Other data.} Several datasets from TURKSTAT are used to construct controls and deflators. I use the 3-digit sectoral Producer Price Index (PPI) to deflate nominal variables, and the personnel costs, valued-added factor costs by 4-digit sectors from the Annual Industry and Service Statistics to construct sectoral labor share.

\subsection{Chinese import competition}

Estimating the effects of the change of China import penetration rate on firm outcomes is biased due to several reasons. First, Turkish industry-level demand shocks can affect both imports from China and firm outcomes, creating simultaneity bias. Second, persistent industry characteristics may jointly shape import exposure and firm performance, leading to reverse causality. Third, measurement error arises because firm outcomes are measured at the firm level while import exposure is at the industry-year level, potentially attenuating estimates.

To address these issues, I implement a shift-share instrumental variable (IV) strategy, following \textcite{autorTradeAdjustmentWorkerLevel2014} and the "many exogenous shifts" approach of \textcite{borusyakQuasiExperimentalShiftShareResearch2022}. The key idea is to construct an instrument for the change in Chinese import penetration that captures exogenous variation in Chinese export growth to high-income markets (European Union, the US, Japan, Australia), weighted by predetermined sectoral shares of Chinese import penetration in Turkey. This approach leverages the fact that Chinese export growth to these markets is driven by global demand and China's own supply-side dynamics, which are plausibly exogenous to Turkish industry-specific shocks. By using predetermined shares, I ensure that the instrument is not mechanically correlated with contemporaneous firm-level outcomes. 

Formally, let $M_{jt}^{\text{CHN} \to \text{HI}}$ denote Chinese exports to high-income countries in sector $j$ at time $t$, and let $M_{jt}^{\text{World} \to \text{HI}}$ denote total world exports to high-income countries in the same sector and period. The shift component of the instrument is defined as the change in China’s export share in high-income destination markets:
\[
g_{jt} = \Delta \ln \text{Import Share}_{jt}^{\text{CHN} \to \text{HI}} = \Delta \ln M_{jt}^{\text{CHN} \to \text{HI}} - \Delta \ln M_{jt}^{\text{World} \to \text{HI}}
\]
The share component is given by the baseline level of Chinese import penetration in Turkey in period $t_0$, following (1). The shift-share instrument for changes in Chinese import penetration is therefore constructed as: $Z_{jt}^{\text{Output}} = s_{j,\text{CHN}}^{t_0} \times g_{jt}$. To capture the input-supply channel separately, I construct an additional control variable by weighting the sector-level output instrument by input-output linkages across sectors, following \textcite{amitiTradeLiberalizationIntermediate2007}:
\[
Z_{jt}^{\text{Input}} = \sum_k w_{jk}^{t_0} \times Z_{kt}^{\text{Output}}
\]
where $w_{jk}^{t_0}$ denotes the technical coefficient derived from the 2003 Turkish input-output table, defined as the share of sector $k$ in total intermediate inputs used by sector $j$ at the baseline period $t_0$:
\[
w_{jk}^{t_0} = \frac{\text{Domestic Use}_{jk} + \text{Imported Use}_{jk}}{\text{Total Intermediate Inputs}_j}
\]


\subsection{Markup estimation and other variables}

\textbf{Markups.} To estimate markup levels, I follow the production approach pioneered by \textcite{hallRelationPriceMarginal1988} and further developed by \textcite{deloeckerMarkupsFirmLevelExport2012}. This method relies on estimating the output elasticity of a flexible input from a production function, and then using the ratio of revenue to that input to infer markups. Formally:$$\mu_{it} = \theta_{ivt} \frac{P_{it}Q_{it}}{P_{vit}V_{it}}$$
where $\theta_{ivt}$ is the elasticity of firm $i$'s output to input $v$ at time $t$, $P_{it}Q_{it}$ is firm revenue, and $P_{vit}V_{it}$ is the expenditure on input $v$. The cost shares are directly observed from the firm's financial statements, and the output elasticity is estimated from production function estimation. The choice of input $v$ is crucial: it should be flexible and variable in the short run, such as cost of goods sold (COGS), to ensure that the markup estimation captures the relevant margin. I discuss the choice of input and potential measurement issues in more detail in Appendix A1.1.

Following \textcite{deridderHitchhikersGuideMarkup2026}, I focus on markup adjustments over time rather than levels, to mitigate concerns about measurement error and unobserved heterogeneity in revenue-based markup levels. The change in log markup is defined as:
\[
\Delta \log \hat{\mu}_{it} \equiv \log \hat{\mu}_{it} - \log \hat{\mu}_{i,t-1}
\]

\textbf{Labor Intensity.} Labor intensity is constructed as the ratio of personnel costs to value added factor cost, averaged over 2009--2015 at the 4-digit sector level. This time-invariant measure captures differences in production technology across industries. The interaction of labor intensity with a post-2016 dummy captures differential effects after the minimum wage hike, which may have caused attrition of formal firms from the ORBIS database.

\textbf{Sectoral Markup.} Following \textcite{bursteinBottomUpMarkupFluctuations2025}, sectoral markup is defined as the market-share-weighted harmonic mean of firm markups:
\[
\mu_{jt} = \left( \sum_{i \in j} s_{ijt} \mu_{it}^{-1} \right)^{-1}
\]
where $s_{ijt} = \frac{D_{it}}{\sum_{i' \in j} D_{i't}}$ is firm market share, which is firm domestic sales deflated by industry PPI. The harmonic mean is appropriate because it gives more weight to low-markup firms, which are more likely to be affected by import competition and selection. The change in sectoral markup is then defined as the first difference of the log of the sectoral markup.

\section{Empirical strategy}
My empirical strategy combines four components, each designed to address a specific aspect of the identification and estimation challenges in this context. First, I implement a shift-share instrumental variable (IV) approach to identify the causal effect of Chinese import penetration on firm-level markup adjustments, while separating the output-competition channel from the input-supply channel. Second, I correct for endogenous firm exit using a semiparametric selection-correction method motivated by dynamic firm models. Third, I decompose changes in sectoral markups into within-firm and reallocation components to connect micro-level responses to aggregate outcomes. Finally, I analyze heterogeneous effects across the distribution of markup adjustments using grouped IV quantile regression. 

\subsection{Identification strategy and baseline IV regression}

My baseline IV specification estimates the effect of the change in Chinese import penetration on firm-level markup adjustments, controlling for the input supply channel and other covariates. The regression is specified as follows:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
Y_{ijt} &= \beta_O \Delta IP_{jt} + \beta_I Z_{jt}^{\text{Input}} + \gamma_1 \text{LaborShare}_{jt_0} \times \text{Post2016}_t \\
&\quad + \gamma_2 \text{LaborShare}_{jt_0} + X'_{ijt-1} \gamma + \tau_t + \delta_j + \varepsilon_{ijt}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

where $Y_{ijt}$ is the outcome of interest (exit probability or markup adjustments), $\Delta IP_{jt}$ is the change in Chinese import penetration, $Z_{jt}^{\text{Input}}$ is the control variable for the input supply channel, $\text{LaborShare}_{jt_0}$ is the pre-2016 averaged labor intensity, $X_{ijt-1}$ are lagged firm-level controls, and $\tau_t$ and $\delta_j$ are time and sector fixed effects. The key coefficient of interest is $\beta_O$, which captures the effect of import competition on firm outcomes, while controlling for input supply effects and other confounders.

To identify $\beta_O$, I use the shift-share output-competition IV $Z_{jt}^{\text{Output}}$ for $\Delta IP_{jt}$, and include $Z_{jt}^{\text{Input}}$ as a control to isolate the output competition effect. The share is the 2009 exposure share. In addition, the control for pre-2016 labor share interacted with the post-2016 dummy captures potential differential effects after the minimum wage hike, which may have caused attrition of formal firms \parencite{akcigitFactsBusinessDynamism2020} from the ORBIS database (see Appendix for the figure of number of firms over time, need to be added)

To assess the plausibility of the exclusion restriction, I conduct lead-placebo tests. If the instrument is valid, future realizations of the shock should not predict current outcomes once fixed effects and controls are included. In contrast, if there are pre-trends or if the instrument is correlated with unobserved determinants of firm outcomes, we would expect to see significant coefficients on leads of the instrument.

Formally, for horizon $k \in \{1,2,3\}$, I estimate:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
Y_{ijt} &= \beta^{k}_O Z^{\text{Output}}_{j,t+k} + \beta_I0 Z_{jt}^{\text{Input}} + \beta_{I1} Z_{j,t-1}^{\text{Input}} + \gamma_1 \text{LaborShare}_{jt_0} \times \text{Post2016}_t \\
&\quad + \gamma_2 \text{LaborShare}_{jt_0} + X'_{ijt-1} \gamma + \tau_t + \delta_j + \varepsilon_{ijt}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Under the identifying assumption, the placebo coefficients $\beta^{k}_O$ should be statistically indistinguishable from zero, providing evidence against pre-trends and supporting the validity of the instrument. I summarize these placebo coefficients graphically using a coefficient plot with confidence intervals across horizons.

\subsection{Selection correction}

A key identification concern in the baseline regression is endogenous sample selection. Firm-level markup adjustments are observed only for firms that survive and continue reporting in the sample. If Chinese import competition affects the probability of firm exit, and if exit is correlated with unobserved firm characteristics that also shape pricing behavior, then the baseline IV estimates conflate a within-firm response of surviving firms with compositional changes induced by selective attrition. 

To address this issue, I adopt a semiparametric control-function correction inspired by the dynamic firm literature of \textcite{ericsonMarkovPerfectIndustryDynamics1995} and the selection correction approach of \textcite{backusWhyProductivityCorrelated2020}. The point is not to re-estimate the exit problem here, but to purge the markup equation of selection on unobservables: if survival depends on latent firm state and that state also affects markup adjustment, then the regression error among observed firms is not mean-independent of trade exposure.

I proxy the latent state with lagged EBIT margin, denoted $s_{i,t-1}$. This variable is observable, persistent, predictive of exit, and informative about subsequent markup adjustment, so it provides a practical control-function index for selection. Let the second-stage error decompose as $\varepsilon_{i(j)t} = \lambda_{jt} + \eta_{it}$, where $\lambda_{jt}$ captures sector-year shocks and $\eta_{it}$ is an idiosyncratic component. Among surviving firms, I model the selection-induced component as a flexible function of the lagged state proxy,
\[
\mathbb{E}[\eta_{it} \mid s_{it-1}] = g(s_{it-1}),
\]
and approximate $g(\cdot)$ with a third-order polynomial in $s_{it-1}$. The corrected second-stage regression therefore includes this control function:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\Delta \ln \mu_{i(j)t} &= \beta_O \widehat{\Delta IP_{jt}} + \beta_I Z_{jt}^{\text{Input}} + \gamma_1 \text{LaborShare}_{jt_0} \times \text{Post2016}_t \\
&\quad + \gamma_2 \text{LaborShare}_{jt_0} + X'_{ijt-1} \gamma + \tau_t + \delta_j + g(s_{it-1}) + \epsilon_{i(j)t}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Conditional on the flexible control function $g(s_{it-1})$, the remaining variation in the instrumented import penetration shock identifies the causal within-firm effect on markup adjustments, net of selection bias. Thus, $\beta_O$ should be interpreted as a within-incumbent treatment effect rather than a reduced-form combination of treatment and selection.

\subsection{Decomposition of sectoral markup changes}

Firm-level regressions identify whether Chinese import penetration disciplines incumbents' pricing behavior, but they do not by themselves show how these responses aggregate to the sector level. This matters because sectoral markups may move differently from firm-level markups when import shocks also reallocate market shares across incumbents or induce entry and exit. To connect micro responses to sector-wide outcomes, I therefore decompose annual changes in sectoral markups into within-firm, reallocation, entry, and exit components. This decomposition suited to environments with variable markups, where sectoral markup movements depend jointly on firm-specific markup adjustment and changes in market shares. 

Let $S_t$ denote the set of firms active in sector $j$ at time $t$, $C = S_{t-1} \cap S_t$ the set of continuing firms, $E = S_t \setminus S_{t-1}$ entrants, and $X = S_{t-1} \setminus S_t$ exiters. Adapted from \textcite{melitzDynamicOlleyPakesProductivity2015}, the change in sectoral inverse markup can then be written as:
\[\Delta \mu_{jt}^{-1}
=
\underbrace{\sum_{i \in C} \bar{s}_{ij}\,\Delta \mu_{ij}^{-1}}_{\text{Within (continuers)}}
+
\underbrace{\sum_{i \in C} \Delta s_{ij}\,\bar{\mu}_{ij}^{-1}}_{\text{Between (continuers)}}
+
\underbrace{\sum_{i \in E} s_{ijt}\,\mu_{ijt}^{-1}}_{\text{Entry}} - \underbrace{\sum_{i \in X} s_{ij,t-1}\,\mu_{ij,t-1}^{-1}}_{\text{Exit}}\]
where $\bar{s}_{ij} = \frac{1}{2}(s_{ijt} + s_{ijt-1})$ and $\bar{\mu}_{ij}^{-1} = \frac{1}{2}(\mu_{it}^{-1} + \mu_{it-1}^{-1})$ are the average market share and inverse markup for firm $i$ over the two periods. This decomposition allows me to isolate the contributions of different mechanisms to sectoral markup changes.

The within term captures markup adjustment among continuing firms, holding their market shares fixed at midpoint weights. It therefore measure the contribution of incumbent pricing responses to aggregate markup discipline. The reallocation term captures changes in sectoral inverse markups due to shifts in market shares across continuing firms, holding their markups fixed at midpoint values. This term is positive when market shares move toward firms with lower markups and negative when reallocation favors firms with higher markups. The entry term measures the contribution of newly appearing firms in year $t$, while the exit term captures the contribution of firms present in $t-1$ that disappear in $t$. Because the decomposition is written in inverse markups, a positive component corresponds to a decline in sectoral markups, that is, a pro-competitive effect, while a negative component corresponds to an increase in sectoral markups, that is, an anti-competitive effect.

I compute these four components for each sector-year and then use them separately as dependent variables in the sector-level IV specifications. This allows the import-competition shock to affect aggregate markups through distinct channels. Formally, for each outcome $m \in \{ \text{within-firm, reallocation, entry, exit} \}$, I estimate: 
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    Y^m_{jt} &= \beta_O^m \widehat{\Delta IP}_{jt} + \beta_I^m Z_{jt}^{\text{Input}} + \Gamma' W_{jt} + \delta_j + \tau_t + \epsilon_{jt}^m
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $Y^m_{jt}$ is the sector-level outcome corresponding to component $m$, $\widehat{\Delta IP_{jt}}$ is the change in Chinese import penetration instrumented by the output-competition shock, $Z_{jt}^{\text{Input}}$ is the control for input supply channel, $W_{jt}$ are sector-level controls, and $\delta_j$ and $\tau_t$ are sector and year fixed effects. The coefficient $\beta_O^m$ captures the effect of import competition on the specific channel $m$.

This decomposition is central because the aggregate response of sectoral markups need not mirror the average firm-level response. Even if import competition disciplines continuing firms, the sectoral effect may be attenuated or reversed when market shares are reallocated toward dominant incumbents, or when entry and exit contribute in offsetting directions. Estimating the four terms separately therefore makes it possible to distinguish whether sectoral markup changes are primarily driven by incumbent adjustments, reallocation of market shares, or selection through entry and exit. In addition, to assess the relative importance of each margin, I express each decomposition component as a share of the total absolute change in inverse sector markup. 

\subsection{Distributional effects and concentration heterogeneity}

To move beyond average effects, I examine whether the impact of import competition varies across the distribution of firm-level markup adjustments. This is important because mean IV estimates may mask whether trade discipline is concentrated among firms at the upper tail of the markup-adjustment distribution, where pricing power is likely to be strongest. I therefore complement the baseline specification with a grouped IV quantile approach \textcite{chetverikovIVQuantileRegression2016} adapted to the settings in which the endogenous treatment varies at the sector-year level. 

I define $\rho_{jt}^{(k)}$ as the $k$-th decile of the distribution of the within-sector-year distribution of firm-level markup adjustments among survivors. For each decile $k$, the regression is specified as:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\rho_{jt}^{(k)} &= \beta_O^{(k)} \widehat{\Delta IP_{jt}} + \beta_I^{(k)} Z_{jt}^{\text{Input}} + \gamma_1^{(k)} \text{LaborShare}_{jt_0} \times \text{Post2016}_t \\
&\quad + \gamma_2^{(k)} \text{LaborShare}_{jt_0} + X'_{jt-1} \gamma^{(k)} + \tau_t^{(k)} + \delta_j^{(k)} + \nu_{jt}^{(k)}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

The grouped formulation is preferable to a standard firm-level IV quantile regression because the treatment is common within sector-year cells. Pooling firms directly would require rank-similarity assumptions across sectors that are difficult to justify in the presence of sector-level unobservables. By using sector-year quantiles as the dependent variable, the specification instead isolates how import competition shifts different parts of the outcome distribution across groups. 

Identification relies on the same shift-share design as in the baseline analysis. Sectoral changes in Chinese import penetration are instrumented using exogenous growth in Chinese exports to high-income destinations interacted with predetermined Turkish exposure shares, while controlling for the input supply channel. Conditional on fixed effects and controls, $\widehat{\Delta IP_{jt}}$ is therefore interpreted as exogenous variation in output-market competitive pressure. 

Because sample quantiles are mechanically sensitive to the number of firms in each sector-year cell, particularly in smaller sectors, I correct for order-statistic bias (Neyman and Scott, 1948) by augmenting the specification with a flexible control function of the number of firm observations $n_{jt}$:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\rho_{jt}^{(k)} &= \beta_O^{(k)} \widehat{\Delta IP_{jt}} + \beta_I^{(k)} Z_{jt}^{\text{Input}} + \gamma_1^{(k)} \text{LaborShare}_{jt_0} \times \text{Post2016}_t \\
&\quad + \gamma_2^{(k)} \text{LaborShare}_{jt_0} + X'_{jt-1} \gamma^{(k)} + \tau_t^{(k)} + \delta_j^{(k)} + g^{(k)}(n_{jt}) + \nu_{jt}^{(k)}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $g^{(k)}(\cdot)$ is approximated with a third-order polynomial in $n_{jt}$. This follows the correction proposed in \textcite{backusWhyProductivityCorrelated2020}, where order statistics can be spuriously correlated with group size when the number of firms is small relative to the number of quantiles.

I then study heterogeneity in the pro-competitive effect by interacting predicted import penetration with lagged domestic concentration measures such as HHI, CR10, and CR4:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\rho_{jt}^{(k)} &= \beta_{O1}^{(k)} \widehat{\Delta IP_{jt}} + \beta_{O2}^{(k)} \widehat{\Delta IP_{jt}} \times \text{Concentration}_{jt-1} + \beta_I^{(k)} Z_{jt}^{\text{Input}}  + \gamma_2^{(k)} \text{LaborShare}_{jt_0} \\
&\quad + \gamma_1^{(k)} \text{LaborShare}_{jt_0} \times \text{Post2016}_t + X'_{jt-1} \gamma^{(k)} + \tau_t^{(k)} + \delta_j^{(k)} + g^{(k)}(n_{jt}) + \nu_{jt}^{(k)}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

This specification tests whether import competition exerts stronger disciplinary effects in sectors where domestic market power is initially more concentrated. The same logic is extended to additional mechanism tests using interactions with baseline sectoral characteristics, such as initial markup levels, size, or revenue growth.

\section{Results}

\subsection{First stage and identification diagnostics}

I begin by assessing the relevance of the output-side instrument and the empirical distinctness of the output and input channels. Table \ref{tab:firststage} reports the first-stage estimates from regressing sectoral Chinese import penetration on the output-competition instrument \(Z^{\text{Output}}_{jt}\), while controlling for the input-supply shifter \(Z^{\text{Input}}_{jt}\), sector fixed effects, and year fixed effects. I report both a parsimonious specification and the baseline specification that additionally includes lagged firm-level controls.

The first-stage results show that the instrument is strongly predictive of realized import-penetration changes. In both specifications, the coefficient on \(Z^{\text{Output}}_{jt}\) is positive and precisely estimated, with an estimate of 0.892 and a standard error of 0.179. The corresponding excluded-instrument first-stage \(F\)-statistic is about 24.8 in both columns, well above conventional weak-instrument thresholds. This indicates that the variation in Chinese export growth to other high-income destinations provides a strong source of identifying variation for import competition in Turkish manufacturing sectors.

By contrast, the coefficient on the input-supply control \(Z^{\text{Input}}_{jt}\) is small and statistically insignificant in the first stage. This pattern is useful for interpretation. It suggests that, conditional on sector and year fixed effects, the main predictive power for sectoral import penetration comes from the output-side shift-share instrument rather than from the upstream input channel. In other words, the first stage is not being driven by input-cost comovement.

A related concern is whether the two shifters are too closely aligned to be interpreted as distinct channels. The table therefore also reports the small correlation between \(Z^{\text{Output}}_{jt}\) and \(Z^{\text{Input}}_{jt}\). This positive but modest correlation indicates that the two measures are related, as one would expect given their common connection to China-driven trade shocks, but far from collinear. In the robustness, orthogonalizing the output shock out of the input supply channel does not change the any coefficients of interest, which suggests that all findings are robust to this specification. This supports the strategy of using \(Z^{\text{Output}}_{jt}\) as the excluded instrument while including \(Z^{\text{Input}}_{jt}\) as a control for the input-supply channel.

Finally, the near-identical first-stage estimates across the parsimonious and baseline specifications indicate that the predictive content of the instrument is not sensitive to the inclusion of lagged firm characteristics. This stability is reassuring for the subsequent IV analysis. Overall, the first-stage evidence supports the relevance of the output-side instrument and suggests that the identifying variation used in the baseline specifications is both strong and economically interpretable.

At the same time, column (1) shows that import competition also affects firm survival. The positive and significant coefficient in the exit regression indicates that sectors more exposed to Chinese import penetration experience higher firm exit rates. This matters for identification because the observed sample of markup changes is conditional on survival. If import competition disproportionately removes firms with systematically different markup dynamics, conventional IV estimates on surviving firms may conflate within-firm pricing responses with composition effects arising from endogenous selection.

To address this issue, column (4) implements the selection-correction procedure based on a control function in the spirit of Backus (2020), using lagged EBIT margin as a proxy for the latent firm state governing survival and subsequent adjustment decisions. The corrected estimate remains negative and significant, implying that the decline in markup adjustments is not solely an artifact of sample selection. Hence, even after accounting for endogenous survival, the evidence supports a genuine within-firm disciplining effect of import competition on markups.

Comparing columns (3) and (4) further suggests that selection bias is economically relevant but not dominant. Once lagged firm controls are included, the coefficient on import penetration is $-0.462$; after adding the polynomial control-function correction, it becomes $-0.454$. The stability of the estimate indicates that selection does not overturn the baseline conclusion, although it remains important to account for it explicitly when firm exit responds to the same trade shock. Put differently, Chinese import competition appears to reduce markup growth among surviving firms, while also reshaping the composition of firms that remain in the sample.

Finally, the coefficient on the input-supply shock is statistically insignificant in all markup specifications. This suggests that, in this setting, the output-competition channel is the main force behind firm-level pricing adjustment, whereas upstream input-supply effects do not generate a robust independent effect on markup changes. Substantively, the results imply that Chinese import exposure primarily disciplines domestic firms through direct competitive pressure in output markets rather than through changes in access to imported inputs.

\subsection{From firm to sector}

Table \ref{tab:decomposition_results_alt} connects the firm-level evidence to sector-level markup dynamics by decomposing changes in inverse sector markups into four components: within-firm adjustment, reallocation across continuing firms, entry, and exit. The sectoral markup is constructed as the inverse of the market-share-weighted average of firm-level inverse markups, so that changes in sectoral market power reflect both individual pricing responses and changes in the composition of firms and market shares. This aggregation follows the harmonic-mean logic emphasized in the recent markup literature.

Panel A shows that, under the comparison specification, the estimated effect of output import competition on the total change in sector-level markups is small and statistically insignificant. The same is true for each individual component. In particular, the coefficient on within-firm adjustment is close to zero, while the reallocation and entry margins are negative and the exit margin is positive, but all are imprecisely estimated. Taken together, these estimates suggest that, once firm-level responses are aggregated to the sector level, the pro-competitive effect of Chinese import penetration does not translate into a precisely estimated change in sectoral markups. By contrast, the input-supply shock is associated with a significant increase in inverse sector markups changes, which is equivalent to lower sectoral markup growth. This effect is driven almost entirely by the reallocation component: sectors more exposed to upstream input shocks experience a statistically significant shift in market shares toward firms with higher inverse markups, or lower markups. The within-firm, entry, and exit terms are all statistically insignificant.

\subsection{Distributional heterogeneity}

Average effects can conceal substantial heterogeneity in firms' responses to import competition. To examine how the trade shock affects different parts of the markup-adjustment distribution, I estimate grouped IV quantile regressions at the sector-year level. The results reveal a clear asymmetric pattern. Across the central part of the distribution, from the 10th to roughly the 70th percentile, the estimated effects of Chinese import competition on markup adjustments are close to zero. By contrast, the coefficients become increasingly negative from the upper quartile onward, reaching about $-2.0$ at the 90th percentile. Figure \ref{fig:ivq_combined} shows that the pro-competitive effect is therefore concentrated in the upper tail of the distribution rather than spread uniformly across firms.

This pattern suggests that import competition primarily constrains large positive markup adjustments among firms located at the top of the distribution. In other words, the main within-firm discipline effect does not operate through a broad compression of all firms' pricing behavior, but through a disproportionate reduction in the ability of upper-tail firms to raise markups. Firms in the center of the distribution, which are already making small or moderate markup adjustments, appear much less responsive to the trade shock. This finding is consistent with models in which competition bites most strongly for firms enjoying greater ex ante pricing power.

I next examine whether this upper-tail compression depends on domestic market structure. To do so, I interact import penetration with a measure of pre-period sectoral concentration, proxied by the domestic CR4 ratio. The estimates, reported in Figure \ref{fig:ivq_combined}, show that the negative upper-tail effects are stronger in more concentrated sectors. The interaction coefficients remain close to zero in the lower and central quantiles, but become increasingly negative at high quantiles when concentration is high. This indicates that the compression of the markup-adjustment distribution is amplified in markets where a small number of firms account for a large share of domestic sales.

Taken together, these results support a mechanism in which import competition disciplines the firms with the greatest scope to adjust markups upward, especially in concentrated domestic industries. The evidence is therefore more consistent with targeted compression at the top of the distribution than with a uniform decline in markups across all firms. This also helps reconcile the firm-level and sector-level results: even when average sectoral effects appear modest, import competition may still exert economically meaningful pro-competitive pressure by restricting the pricing behavior of high-markup firms.

\section{Robustness checks}

\subsection{Identification robustness}

Table \ref{tab:rob12_fe} reports robustness checks of the main IV estimate using alternative functional forms and progressively reduced control sets. In the baseline specification (column 1), import penetration has a negative and statistically significant effect on markup growth (coefficient: -0.462), and this result remains significant under wild-cluster bootstrap inference (p = 0.026). Adding a quadratic term for import penetration (column 2) does not provide evidence of meaningful nonlinearity, as the squared term is imprecisely estimated. Across columns 3 to 9, the coefficient on import penetration remains consistently negative and broadly similar in magnitude as controls are removed step-by-step, indicating that the main result is not driven by a specific control choice. Overall, the table supports the robustness of the core finding that greater import penetration is associated with lower markup adjustments.

Rotemberg weights are concentrated, which is consistent with the granular structure of Turkish manufacturing and the oligopolistic mechanism emphasized in the model. Excluding the most influential sectors sharply weakens the first stage, indicating that the identifying variation is economically concentrated rather than broadly diffuse (Table \ref{tab:rob_rotemberg_exclusions}).


\subsection{Alternative markup measures}
I implement the \textcite{kirovMeasuringMarkupsRevenue2026} methodology (Appendix A.2) as a robustness check to the baseline measurement. Table \ref{tab:kirov_iv} reports these estimates. Because this approach requires estimating markup levels using lagged markup shifters and a Markov process for revenue productivity, the usable firm-year sample falls substantially relative to the baseline. When markup adjustments are then constructed from these estimated levels, the sample contracts further in a non-random way (about 25\% sample loss). Therefore, while the estimation is not statistically significant, the point estimates remain negative and of similar magnitude to the baseline, suggesting that the main results are not driven by the specific markup construction method. 

After re-estimation with tighter or looser trimming of extreme markup changes does not alter the sign or magnitude of the main coefficient (Table \ref{tab:rob52_trimming}), which suggests that the results are not driven by outliers in the markup-adjustment distribution. The IV quantile estimates are also robust to alternative trimming thresholds, with the same pattern of negative effects concentrated in the upper tail (Appendix Figure \ref{fig:rob52_trimming_ivq}).

\subsection{Selection and panel composition}
Restricting the sample to long-lived firms attenuates the estimated effect and reduces precision. This is expected: conditioning on survival removes part of the adjustment margin operating through firm turnover and sample composition. The baseline specification, which uses the full unbalanced panel, is therefore more appropriate for capturing the aggregate pro-competitive effect of import competition.

\subsection{Residualizing the input supply channel}
As an additional check, I residualize the output-competition instrument with respect to the input-supply shock and re-estimate the main specifications using only the orthogonalized component of the output shock. This addresses the concern that the main IV could be picking up variation shared with the input channel rather than purely output-market competitive pressure. Appendix Tables \ref{tab:rob_resid_fs}--\ref{tab:rob_resid_aux} report the first-stage comparison, the baseline markup specification, the control-set sensitivity exercise, and the auxiliary-outcome regressions.

The results are strikingly stable. In the baseline markup equation, the coefficient on import penetration is unchanged when the residualized instrument replaces the original output IV: the point estimate remains $-0.462$ with the same standard error and the same first-stage strength (Appendix Table \ref{tab:rob_resid_main}). The same stability appears in the auxiliary outcomes. Revenue growth and domestic-sales growth remain negative and statistically significant, while export growth remains negative but imprecisely estimated (Appendix Table \ref{tab:rob_resid_aux}). In other words, purging the output instrument of its linear overlap with $Z^{\text{Input}}$ does not alter the qualitative or quantitative conclusions.

This robustness check strengthens the interpretation of the main estimates as evidence for an output-competition channel. The negative markup response is not an artifact of correlation between the two shift-share shocks; rather, it survives when identification comes only from the component of output competition orthogonal to the input-supply shock. Together with the mechanism exercises, this supports the view that Chinese import exposure primarily disciplined firms through product-market competition rather than through contamination from the input-cost channel.

\subsection{Distributional design robustness}
I report three additional distributional checks in Appendix Figure \ref{fig:rob41_decquin}--\ref{fig:rob43_thin}. First, comparing grouped IVQ estimates built on deciles versus quintiles yields very similar profiles: coefficients are close to zero through the middle of the distribution and turn more negative in the upper tail, indicating that the main pattern is not driven by bin choice (Figure \ref{fig:rob41_decquin}). Second, I re-rank observations by lagged markup level instead of markup change. The resulting coefficients remain weak in most bins and become most negative near the top ranks, confirming that upper-tail discipline is not specific to the baseline ranking metric (Figure \ref{fig:rob42_rank}). Third, I exclude thin sector-year cells and compare cutoffs for minimum cell size. The estimated quantile profile is nearly unchanged across these restrictions, with the strongest negative effects still concentrated at high quantiles (Figure \ref{fig:rob43_thin}).

\section{Conclusion}

This thesis studies how Chinese import competition reshaped market power in Turkish manufacturing by separating output-competition from input-supply effects and by distinguishing within-firm markup adjustment from compositional change. Using ORBIS/AMADEUS firm data, a shift-share instrument based on Chinese exports to other high-income markets, an input-supply shifter built from input-output linkages, and complementary evidence from selection correction, grouped IV quantiles, and sectoral decomposition, I find a clear pro-competitive effect at the firm level. Higher Chinese import penetration reduces markup growth among surviving firms, with stronger effects in the upper tail of the markup-adjustment distribution and in more concentrated sectors. The input-supply channel does not robustly raise within-firm markups, but it contributes to reallocation toward lower-markup firms in sectors more exposed to Chinese inputs.

These findings qualify a simple view of trade liberalization as either pure discipline or pure creative destruction. Import competition increases exit probabilities, yet the selection correction shows that the within-firm disciplining effect is not driven by survival bias. At the sector level, entry and exit explain little of overall markup adjustment relative to within-firm changes and reallocation across continuing firms. The Turkish case is therefore best described as discipline without strong creative destruction: foreign competition compresses incumbents' pricing power, but its aggregate effect on market power depends on how market shares are redistributed among survivors. More broadly, the thesis contributes causal evidence from an emerging economy, disentangles output-market discipline from input-cost relief, and shows that average effects conceal substantial heterogeneity in firms' responses.

Several limitations remain. Markups are inferred from accounting data and therefore depend on production-function assumptions and sample coverage, while the reduced-form design cannot fully trace sourcing changes, product prices, or longer-run innovation dynamics. Future research could combine customs, product-price, and employment data to link markup compression to consumer prices, wages, and welfare, and could extend the analysis to long-run entry, productivity, and cross-country comparisons.

\section{Empirical Appendix}

\subsection{Markup Estimation}

\subsubsection{Setup}

I estimate firm markups using the production approach \parencite{deridderHitchhikersGuideMarkup2026,deloeckerMarkupsFirmLevelExport2012}. For firm $i$ in year $t$, define:
\begin{align*}
r_{it} &= \log R_{it} \quad \text{(log revenue)} \\
x_{it} &= \log X_{it} \quad \text{(log COGS)} \\
k_{it} &= \log K_{it} \quad \text{(log capital)}
\end{align*}

Sample restrictions: $R_{it} > 0$, $X_{it} > 0$, $K_{it} > 0$.

For a flexible (variable) input, the production approach gives:
\[
\mu_{it} = \alpha_{it}^{\text{COGS}} \cdot \frac{R_{it}}{X_{it}}
\]

In logs:
\[
\log \hat{\mu}_{it} = \log \hat{\alpha}_{it}^{\text{COGS}} + \log R_{it} - \log X_{it}
\]

\subsubsection{Estimating the Output Elasticity of COGS}

\paragraph{Cobb--Douglas (constant elasticity)}

Estimate within ISIC4:
\[
r_{it} = \beta_x x_{it} + \beta_k k_{it} + \lambda_t + \varepsilon_{it}
\]

Then $\hat{\alpha}_{it}^{\text{COGS}} = \hat{\beta}_x$.

\paragraph{Two-input Translog (elasticity varies with input levels)}

Estimate:
\[
r_{it} = \beta_x x_{it} + \beta_k k_{it} + \beta_{xx} x_{it}^2 + \beta_{kk} k_{it}^2 + \beta_{xk} x_{it} k_{it} + \lambda_t + \varepsilon_{it}
\]

Then the firm-year COGS elasticity is:
\[
\hat{\alpha}_{it}^{\text{COGS}} = \frac{\partial r_{it}}{\partial x_{it}} = \hat{\beta}_x + 2\hat{\beta}_{xx} x_{it} + \hat{\beta}_{xk} k_{it}
\]

\subsection{Implementing Kirov et al. (2026) methodology as robustness check}

KMT is designed to treat omitted-price bias and allow markup heterogeneity across firms and over time. KMT methodology consists of two steps: first, recover a revenue elasticity, which is contaminated by the markup, from the flexible-input share equation; second, use a Markov process for revenue productivity to back out the true physical output elasticity and thus the markup level. The KMT approach is more robust to misspecification of the production function and allows for more flexible markup dynamics, but it also relies on stronger assumptions about the evolution of productivity and the structure of demand.

\textbf{Revenue production function}: I would use a Cobb-Douglas baseline: 
\[r_{it} = \beta_x x_{it} + \beta_k k_{it} + \nu_{it} + \varepsilon_{it}\]
where $\nu_{it}$ is the revenue productivity shock that follows a first-order Markov process: $\nu_{it} = \rho \nu_{it-1} + \eta_{it}$, and $\varepsilon_{it}$ is an i.i.d. shock. The key difference from the standard production function estimation is that the dependent variable is revenue rather than physical output, which means that the estimated $\beta_x$ captures both the physical output elasticity with respect to the flexible input and the markup. 

\textbf{Revenue productivity Markov process}: To identify the physical output elasticity, KMT exploits the first-order Markov structure of revenue productivity as follows:
\[\nu_{it} = \rho g(\nu_{it-1}) + \eta_{it}\]
where $g(\cdot)$ is a non-parametric function that captures the potentially non-linear evolution of revenue productivity. I approximate $g(\cdot)$ using a third-order polynomial in lagged revenue productivity.

\textbf{Markup control function}: Let markups be a function of inputs, time and firm fixed effects, and a vector of both firm- and sector-level time-varying parameters $\mathbb{D}_{i(j)t-1}$ that shift markups but are not included in the production function estimation. These parameters include lagged share of sales, lagged exporter status, and lagged HHI at the ISIC4 level, which are all potential confounders of the markup estimation. 

\textbf{Share equation}: I then regress the log flexible-input cost share in revenue ($s^x_{it} \equiv cx_{it} - r_{it}$) on log inputs and markup shifters:
\[s^x_{it} = \ln \beta_x - \mu_{it} + b - \varepsilon_{it}\]
where $\ln(\beta_x)$ is the technological component, which captures the effect of the flexible input on revenue. $\mu_{it}$ is the markup component, which captures the effect of markups on the cost share. Higher markups lower the cost share, so it enters with a negative sign. $b-\varepsilon_{it}$ is the error term, which states that even after firms choose inputs, realized cost shares may differ from the predicted cost shares due to measurement error or other unobserved factors.

In my analysis, the share equation is estimated as follows:
\[\ln(\text{COGS}_{it}) - \ln(R_{it}) = \ln{\beta_{COGS}} - \big(\phi_0 + \phi_x x_{it} + \phi_k k_{it} + \phi_1 \text{share\_sales}_{it-1} + \phi_2 \text{exporter\_status}_{it-1} + \phi_3 \text{HHI}_{jt-1} + \tau_t + \delta_j + \lambda_i \big) + b - \varepsilon_{it}\]

\textbf{Final markup estimator}: After recovering $\hat{\theta}^{\text{COGS}} = \hat{\beta}_{\text{COGS}}$, I compute: 
\[\hat{\mu}_{it} = \frac{\hat{\theta}_{\text{COGS}}}{\text{COGS}_it / R_{it}}\]
then define the markup adjustment as $\Delta \hat{\mu}_{it} = \hat{\mu}_{it} - \hat{\mu}_{it-1}$, which is the dependent variable in the main regression analysis.
